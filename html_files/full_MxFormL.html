Temporal bounds[edit]

♠ Duration ♣ 400-101 BCE ♥ [7] The following refers to a previous periodization. The start date 650 BC for the MxFormL quasi-polity is the beginning of the Late Formative period (c.650-200 BC) in the Basin of Mexico (alternatively called "First Intermediate Period 2").[8][9][10] The end date is fuzzy and problematic because it is unclear exactly when in the Terminal Formative period (c.200-1 BC; alternatively called "First Intermediate Period 3") the entire MxFormL quasi-polity was conquered by its aggressive neighbors Cuicuilco (Basin of Mexico-Cui) and Teotihuacan (Basin of Mexico-Teo). Since the MxFormL quasi-polity is made up of multiple, discrete, independent settlement clusters, the timing of their conquest by Cuicuilco and Teotihuacan was most likely different for different areas.[11][12][13][14][15][16] This quasi-polity ceased to exist during the subsequent Tzacualli ceramic phase (c.1 BCE - 100 CE) because the Plinian eruption of Popocatepetl led to the abandonment of most of the quasi-polity', while the rest of the quasi-polity was taken over by Teotihuacan and Cuicuilco.[17][18][19] The date ranges for the eruption/abandonment are c. 100 BCE - 50 CE, and the start date for the Tzacualli ceramic transition is centered around 1 BCE, so I use the date of 1 BCE to designate both events.

♠ Degree of centralization ♣ quasi-polity ♥

While some have hypothesized that Cuicuilco headed some kind of Supra-polity political system during the Late Formative,[20][21][22][23] the characteristics of such a Supra-polity political system are unknown to archaeologists.

General Description[edit]

The Basin or Valley of Mexico is a highlands plateau in central Mexico roughly corresponding to modern-day Mexico City. Here, we are interested in the phase of its prehistory known as the Late Formative period (c. 400-101 BCE). In this period, polities throughout Mesoamerica experienced increases in wealth, influence, and hierarchical complexity.[38] In the Basin of Mexico, Cuicuilco, Tlapacoya, and Cholula all became major regional centers with monumental architecture.[39]

No estimates could be found for the population of the average autonomous political unit at the time. The largest known settlement, Cuicuilco, may have had a population of at least 20,000 acrross 400 ha.[40]

Political power was inherently theocratic;[41][42][43] beyond that, the exact administrative mechanisms prevalent at the time remain unclear.

Political and religious institutions are thought to be essentially identical for the Central Mexican Highlands Late/Terminal Formative, such that political power was inherently theocratic.[66][67][68] Beyond indirect, theoretical proxies like hierarchical levels of socioeconomic stratification, irrigation systems, monumental construction, and settlement patterns, there is no direct evidence for sociopolitical administrative levels.

♠ Religious levels ♣ 2 ♥ levels. Political and religious institutions are thought to be essentially identical for the Central Mexican Highlands Late/Terminal Formative, such that political power was inherently theocratic.[69][70][71] At least 3 hierarchical levels of religious institutions can be discerned for Cuicuilco. First, formal religious ritual at the household/house-group level (i.e. local corporate/Kin groups, the the basal units of exchange and production) is ubiquitous for the Central Mexican Highlands Late/Terminal Formative, and these are thought to have organized the basal level of the political economy. This has been inferred from the ubiquitous household/house-group shrines and ritual offerings, which are associated with senior lineage houses in house compounds. Compared to junior lineage houses, senior lineage houses also have higher quantities of prestige goods, food storage, obsidian/lithic production, burials, larger houses/rooms, greater architectural quality and ornamentation, and greater occupational time depth. Given that these same ritual tropes are replicated at higher hierarchical levels, it has been inferred that senior lineage religious-ritual authority in access to ancestors was the basis of political-economic authority at the basal house-group (kin/corporate group) level.[72][73][74][75][76][77][78][79][80][81] Second, formal settlement- or community-level religious rituals (i.e. wider groupings of hierarchically-ordered kin/corporate-groups) were associated with feasting sponsored by religious/political elites, ritual labor service (production/construction), and sacrificial offerings. Evidence for these religious institutions are centered on settlements' ceremonial precincts (temples and plazas), which directly associate them with the priestly elite and the political economy.[82][83][84][85][86][87][88][89] [90]

General Characteristics of Warfare[edit]

Types of Warfare[edit]

♠ Inter-polity wars ♣ unknown: 650-400bce; inferred present: 400bce - 100ce ♥ From about 400-1 BCE, archaeologists believe that the Cuicuilco polity began to take over neighboring subregions (settlement clusters within the MxFormL quasi-polity), including the Tacuba region, Xochimilco, Cuauhtitlan, Chalco, and parts of peninsular Ixtapalapa. Evidence for this includes changes in the spatial distance between sites, major changes in settlement sizes (including Cuicuilco), widespread nucleation, a shift towards hilltop sites, and the abandonment of settlement clusters.[254][255][256][257][258] In addition, the parts of the neighboring Toluca Valley immediately to the west of Cuicuilco (over the mountains) were abruptly abandoned c.400 BCE - 50 CE, with the exception of a handful of sites with Cuicuilco-style pottery.[259] It is possible that Cuicuilco invaded Toluca to maintain access to the Lerma River trade route with west Mexico, similar to Monte Alban's invasion of Cuicatlan Canada.[260][261] Many scholars have also argued that Cuicuilco and Teotihuacan fought wars with each other during the Terminal Formative (c.200 BCE - 100 CE) over supremacy in the Basin of Mexico, which were ultimately won by Teotihuacan following Popocatepetl's Plinian eruption (here set at c.1 BCE), the abandonment of Cuicuilco's territory in the southern Basin of Mexico, and the subsequent decline of Cuicuilco evident in the monumental core. The evidence for this includes the items listed above, as well as the widespread archaeological and iconographic evidence for militarism at early Teotihuacan.[262][263][264][265][266][267][268][269][270][271]

Cultural distance[edit]

♠ Wars within same ethnic group ♣ [present; absent] ♥ Due to the massive population movement from the Formative to the time of the Spanish Contact -- including the widespread displacement of local ethnic groups by Nahuatl-speaking Uto-Aztecans -- and the lack of a deciphered writing system in the Basin of Mexico NGA prior to Nahuatl, the language(s) of Cuicuilco remain unknown.[272][273] Nevertheless, it is widely suggested that the Late/Terminal Formative Basin of Mexico contained a fusion of diverse ethnic groups and languages, which would have varried considerably from region to region (as evidenced by their regional variability in material culture). The architectural, symbolic, and material culture of Cuicuilco have strong ties to both West Mexico[274][275][276][277][278] and the eastern Basin of Mexico and Puebla-Tlaxcala valley.[279][280][281][282][283] The (Proto-)Oto-Manguean language family spread northward into West and Central Mexico with the first farming populations, and it is likely that both Cuicuilco's West and Central Mexican cultural influences were Oto-Manguean speakers.[284][285][286] Nevertheless, Olmec cultural influences in the Basin of Mexico were strong during the Early-Midle Formative and at Teotihuacan (the so-called "Olmeca-Xicallanca" ethnie) -- both of which have strong archaeological similarities with Cuicuilco -- and it has been suggested that these peoples spoke variants of Mixe-Zoquean and/or Totonac languages from the Gulf Coast (based on loan words in local Nahuat dialects).[287][288][289] At present, it is unclear whether Cuicuilco's architectural, symbolic, and material culture similarities with West Mexico are the result of some degree of inmigration or merely strong trade relations, so I have coded these variables as "[present; absent]."
