General description

Our Late Illinois polity refers to historic Illinois Indian society between 1717, the date of the incorporation of the Illinois Country into the French colony of Louisiane,[14] and 1778, when a Revolutionary War skirmish saw the British-held fort of Kaskaskia captured by the Virginia militiaman George Rogers Clark.[15] Until 1717, the French crown had laid claim to the Illinois Country (Pays des Illinois) as part of its North American possessions, but European presence in the region had been generally limited to small numbers of missionaries and traders.[16] Now, however the French made more concerted efforts towards colonization, settling French arable farmers and black slaves in the Middle Mississippi Valley.[17] The Illinois Country became split administratively into two different 'regimes': the Domaine du Roi, the area around the French settlements where French law applied, and the larger 'Indian country', where the Illinois and other groups largely maintained their sovereignty.[18] This page is concerned with 'Indian Country', while the Domaine du Roi falls within our Late Bourbon Kingdom of France polity. In 1763, at the end of the Seven Years' War, France ceded the part of their Louisiana colony that lay east of the Mississippi to the British. The Illinois resisted British incursions into the region for several years but the British eventually managed to occupy Fort de Chartres, the main French military and administrative centre, in 1765, until they too were unseated by George Rogers Clark.[19][20]

Population and political organization

Despite the use of the term 'Illinois Confederacy' to describe Illinois society, there is no indication in the written sources of intertribal political organizations such as those found among Iroquoian groups to the northeast in the same period.[21] Political leadership was provided by both peace chiefs - who played important diplomatic roles, such as conducting calumet (peace pipe) ceremonies for visitors, but had relatively little formal authority - and war chiefs, who organized raids on other settlements.[22][23]
The Illinois Indians living outside of the French settlements along the Mississippi after 1717 CE occupied an ambiguous position under the colonial regime: they were under the 'protection' of the French crown but in practice maintained much of their autonomy and were treated as allies of the French rather than subjects.[24] Nor did the British, after gaining nominal control of the Illinois Country in 1763, make any serious attempts to create agricultural settlements or impose British law on the Indian inhabitants of a region they saw as unprofitable and marginal to their North American empire.[25]
However, the impact of colonization was felt in other ways. The Illinois suffered drastic population losses in the post-contact period, falling from around 12,000 people in 1680 to just 1,900 by 1763.[26]

♠ Name ♣ Calumet ♥ French explorer Allouez provides this description of a Calumet dance he witnessed in 1665-1667: "They practice a kind of dance, quite peculiar to themselves, which they call 'the dance of the tobacco-pipe'. It is executed thus: they prepare a great pipe, which they deck with plumes, and put in the middle of the room, with a sort of veneration. One of the company rises, begins to dance, and then yields his place to another, and this one to a third; and thus they dance in succession, one after another, and not together. One would take this dance for a pantomime ballet; and it is executed to the beating of a drum. The performer makes war in rhythmic time, preparing his arms, atiring himself, running, discovering the foe, raising the cry, slaying the enemy, removing his scalp, and returning home with a song of victory, - and all with an astonishing exactness, promptitude and agility. After they have all danced, one after the other, around the pipe, it is taken and offered to the chief man in the whole assembly, for him to smoke; then to another, and so in succession to all. This ceremony resembles in its significance the French custom of drinking, several out of the same glass; but in addition, the pipe is left in the keeping of the most honored man, as a sacred trust, and a sure pledge of the peace and union that will ever subsist among them as along as it shall remain in that person's hands." ♠ Name ♣ wedding rituals ♥ "In the most formal pattern, a man first approached his father, who during his son's absence on a hunting trip or a raid assembled gifts such as kettles, guns, skins, dried meat, cloth, and perhaps a war captive (Deliette 1934: 331-333). Their quantity indicated his family's wealth as well as their esteem for the woman. Women of the man's family carried these gifts to the lodge of the woman's family. The proposal, made to her father, involved imagery such as a request to warm themselves at their fire or a statement that they had come to seek moccasins. The discussion that followed included the woman and her brothers as well as their parents, who returned the presents if they rejected the offer. If they accepted it, they assembled an equivalent set of gifts and took these, with the woman, to the lodge of her new husband. Such exchanges could continue over four days" [78].

Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ funerary rituals ♥ French explorers and missionaries do not mention rituals performed daily or weekly among the Illinois. Among the rituals they do mention, it seems likely that funerary rituals occurred most frequently. They weren't exceptional events like the Calumet or the Medewiwin, nor were they confined to only a part of the year, like rituals connected to war raids (which only started in February) [114]. Moreover, Illinois population dropped significantly between the seventeenth and the nineteenth century [115], no doubt largely due to almost constant conflict with neighbouring tribes and the impact of European diseases, which suggests that funerary rituals were indeed sadly frequent. "Each sex buried its own dead, painting the face and hair of the corpse red; dressing it in fine clothing; and adding kettles, pots, calumets, bows, food, tobacco, or other items as grave goods [...]. Forked sticks bearing a crosspiece were set at both ends of the grave. Those of distinguished war chiefs were marked by an upright tree trunk, painted to record their exploits. To this was tied a small log for each enemy killed by the person it commemorated. "Ceremonies following death were designed to ensure the spirit's entry into the land of the dead. Most dysphoric collective ritual[edit]

♠ Name ♣ appeasement of Manito ♥ Contemporary documents describe three types of activity that may be described as "dysphoric rituals": vision quests (involving isolation and fasting), raids (involving fear, pain, and risk of death), and a ritual meant to appease the wrath of the god Manito (involving fear and pain). All three are in some way problematic for coding: the first were performed individually rather than collectively; the second, though preceded and followed by ritual, may be too close to the military sphere to be properly described as "rituals"; the third is only mentioned once in the records, in the early nineteenth century, and may therefore have been a one-off (not to mention the fact that many Illinois at the time had converted to Christianity). Overall, however, the appeasement of Manito ritual is the best candidate for coding. In 1811, following an earthquake, "[t]he many camps around Kaskaskia were greatly disturbed and elaborate ceremonies were carried out to appease the visible wrath of Manito. Amidst the wailing and lamentations of the squaws and children the warriors cleansed their hands and faces and prepared for sacrifice to Manito. Deer freshly skinned were hung upon trees with their beads up to heaven. The calumet was smoked with sighing and groans. For three days the men did not speak to women or children and at night lay upon fresh skins with the hair next to the body. No food was taken during this time, All this to provoke dreams which to the Indian was the only mode of communication with Manito. At the end of the three days, the council was held and those who bad had unfavorable dreams appeared with half the face painted black. After the relation of all the dreams, and not until then, did they feast." [170]
