General Description[edit]

The Kingdom of Ghana was the first documented empire of West Africa. Its dominant people, a northern Mande group known as the Soninke, called it 'Wagadu',[33] and Berber traders from the Sahara referred to it as 'Awkar'.[34] Spreading east and north from the Senegal River into modern-day Mauritania and Mali,[35] this polity started growing as a confederation from the 6th century CE.[36] From the 8th century onwards, geographers from North Africa and Spain such as Ibn Hawqal began to document the existence of Ghana in Arabic texts, fantasizing about its gold and resources.[37] The polity reached its peak in the mid-11th century:[38][39] at this stage, its influence spread over Awdaghust (or Aoudaghost) in the Sahara[40] and it encroached on the Niger Inland Delta.[41][42]

Population and political organization

The Wagadu empire comprised four provinces administered by a central government.[43] The king exerted direct authority over his kingdom; he was also head of the traditional religion and was revered as a god.[44] Wagadu society was highly hierarchical, distinguishing between the elite warrior class and the rest of the population: professional artisans including smiths, weavers, dyers and shoemakers; farmers and herders; and slaves.[45] These groups were further subdivided along clan lines.[46]
This period was a prosperous one for the Sudanese region, which produced millet, maize, yam, groundnuts, cotton, indigo and other crops.[47] Linked into a thriving exchange sphere that stretched north to North Africa and the Mediterranean,[48] the Sudanese population exported gold, slaves, hides, and ivory and imported copper, silver beads, dried fruit and cloth.[49] All exports and imports were taxed by the centralized state.[50] Trading outposts in Awdhagust and other Saharan towns facilitated fruitful exchange with Berbers and other groups from further afield.[51]
Population estimates are difficult to obtain for ancient Ghana. However, it is worth noting that its capital, the thriving trading city of Kumbi Saleh, covered 250 hectares and had a population of 15,000-20,000 people at its peak.[52] Archaeological investigations at the site have revealed two-storey stone buildings which may have contained stores on the ground floor, narrow streets with densely packed houses, a mosque, and extensive cemeteries.[53]

Central Bureaucracy[edit]

♠ State-level administrators ♣ inferred present ♥

"Nothing is known about the political methods or history of Ghana under its early kings. What probably happened was that heads of large families or descent-lines among the Soninke, encouraged by the needs and opportunities of the trade in gold and other goods with Berber merchants of the Sahara, saw an advantage in having a single ruler, so they elected a king from among themselves. This king's duty was to organize the trade and keep good relations with the Saharan traders, as well as acting as senior religious leader and as representative on earth of the 'founding ancestors' of the Soninke people. ... He made gifts and gave rewards to all who served him."[474]

The following Mali Empire was like ancient Ghana Empire but on a "greater scale"[475] but did not have a bureaucracy of the extent developed from Askia Mohammed of the Songhai Empire. Likewise the Ghana Empire would not have had a well-developed bureaucracy and what it did have in terms of official administration would have been more rudimentary compared to the Mali state. The Ghana Empire was a less structurally egalitarian society: Ibn Hawqal and Al-Bakri reported servants or slaves of the king would be sacrificed at royal funerals.[476] Islam, which prohibited human sacrifice, was not the religion of state rulers until 1077 CE.[477]

In the Mali state the head of government was called "Mansa"[478] and a military aristocracy formed a council which advised the Emperor.[479] In Ghana the king was known by the title of ghana[480] or by title of kaya maghan which meant 'lord of the gold'.[481] While I have found no specific reference to a governing institution such as an aristocratic council al-Bakri does mention the king's counsellors.

According to al-Bakri when the king of Ghana, under a pavilion, 'gives audience to his people, to listen to their complaints' the 'governor of the city is seated on the ground in front of the king, and all around him are his counsellors in the same position.'[482] When king of Ghana held audience with the people to hear "grievances and remedy them", "sat on the ground before the king and, all about, were the viziers, that is, his ministers, in the same position."[483]

"Ces trois grandes tribus guerrières - les fados ou classe de guerriers - formaient l'aristocratie au sein de laquelle étaient recrutés les hauts fonctonnaires et les grands dignitaires de la cour. Elles constituent la noblesse de race, car toutes sont issues de la grande famille des Wago dont le pays d'origine est Wagadou (il est à noter que la noblesse se dit wagheya)." These three great tribes of warriors - the fados, or warrior class- formed the aristocracy, from which state administrators and court dignitaries came from. They constitute the nobility through breeding, because all come from the Wago family, whose country of origin is Wagadou (it should be noted that the nobility is called wagheya).[484]

While there are no references for the possession of slave tribes by the kings of the ancient Ghana period, which may have been an invention from the following Mali Empire period, there is no reason to believe that in revenue raising terms the state was otherwise much different. In general terms, the West African states were ruled by a non-landowning nobility who lived on "the institution of a tax, first conceived of as a tithe, a ritual deduction on the wealth of all subjects".[485] The state gained revenue from taxation on merchants in gold coins or goods[486] and from customs duties on imports and exports, which had existed from the Ghanaian period.[487]

Was there any court bureaucracy before Songhai?: "It is difficult to realize that, for fifteen hundred years, the Tunkas, Mansas, and Askias simply issued verbal orders and got verbal accounts and reports in return. The customs activities of the border areas, based on what we have just said, would presuppose some kind of precise accounting, just as the payment of taxes and other fees would seem to imply the issuing of receipts, especially to the peripatetic merchants at the markets of Timbuktu, Djenne, etc."[488] Ibn Haukal (10th century) "attests to the use of acknowledgement of debts in writing".[489]

Written language was the culture of an urban élite, that did not absorb surrounding cultures and languages.[490] The development of the bureaucracy in the Western Sudan was linked to arabic writing and the acceptance of Islamic religion. The oldest example of writing in West Africa is from a 1100 CE tomb inscription at Gao.[491] "Islam provided the kind of laws that were useful to bigger states and larger market-towns. Such laws were about the administration of justice, the inheritance of property, the improvement of market organization".[492]

Court ceremony very similar throughout Black Africa: covering head with dust presented a sign of humility.[493] At audiences with the king in ancient Ghana people poured dust over their heads.[494] Only the king wore a headdress at royal audiences.[495] The chief's rank obligated him to speak softly so "the Mansa of Mali, like the Askia, and present-day marabouts, all had their heralds who audibly transmitted to the assembly the words of the chief."[496]

While the later Songhai bureaucracy had officials dressed with a "distinctive uniform and insignia"[497] according to Al Bakri, in Ghana "only the king and his sister's son, that is, the newphew who is heir apparent, were allowed to wear tailored clothes. All others following the same religion as the Tunkara - i.e. the tradition - wore loincloth of cotton, silk, or brocade, according to their means."[498]

Al-Bakri described what might be an incipient bureaucratic center: "The king has a palace and a number of domed dwellings all surrounded with an enclosure like a city wall. ... The king's interpreters, the official in charge of his treasury and the majority of his ministers are Muslims."[499] According to al-Bakri, the king of Ghana could "employ large numbers of messengers and other servants."[500] One of the king's counsellors may have been a finance minister responsible for revenue and taxes. There also may have been officials and translaters to coordinate directly with the foreign merchants whom the king of Ghana permitted residence in a special town.

"the advantages of the long-distance and trans-Saharan trade easily persuaded him to allow the Muslim visitors to build and inhabit a town of their own."[501] Indeed the capital of the Ghana Empire was likely near this town. "In the course of Ghana's long history, the king's capital was undoubtably moved from one place to another."[502] The last capital was Kumbi Saleh, "about 320 kilometres north of modern Bamako. Here too there was a town where the king of Ghana lived, and another town nearby where the Muslim traders had their houses and stables."[503]

The court of the king of Ghana must have had strong links with merchants who sold gold and other goods. Wagadu was a strong commercial state as they were at a north/south trading crossroads on the border of the Sahara. "Their businessmen could obtain gold and ivory from their southern neighbours, and sell these goods to trans-Saharan and North African business partners."[504] Once trade cities which were complex enough to be centers for craftsmen (leather, wood, ivory, metal) established "city governments were needed, as well as men trained to be put in charge of keeping accounts, of maintaining law and order, of looking after the safety of citizens. Then the rulers of these cities began to extend their power to ever wider regions of the neighbouring countryside."[505]

The following Mali Empire had a monopoly on gold production, especially lands of Wangara and Bambuk.[506] Likewise in ancient Ghana the king claimed all gold found in as his own and so monopolised production of gold.[507] More exactly, the king of Ghana "kept for himself only that share of the metal that was found formed into chunks" while "the king left to the people all the gold dust they could extract from the empire's mines."[508]

Deification of Rulers[edit]

♠ Rulers are legitimated by gods ♣ inferred present ♥

In West Africa "What emerges from the records of research, centrally, is that all these peoples awarded supreme power to an idea of God as controlling everything and everyone, but doing this indirectly through subordinate spiritual powers. From this governing concept they derived ... a ruling morality for everyday life: the power of God, they held, would always reward right behaviour and punish wrong behaviour."[742]

"Like the Europeans of the Middle Ages (AD 600-1350), Africans lived in an 'age of faith'. They believed, in short, that political authority came not from men or women but from God and the spirits. Those who exercised power on earth could do so, in other words, only if they were accepted as speaking and acting with the good will of their departed ancestors, who, in turn, were their protectors and helpers in the world of the spirits. Rulers could only rule if they were spiritually appointed to do so; and their subjects obeyed them not simply from respect for the king's power and law, but also for reasons of religion."[743]
