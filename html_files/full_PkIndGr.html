Temporal bounds[edit]

♠ Duration ♣ 180-10 BCE ♥ An independent offshoot of the Greco-Bactrian Kingdom, eventually conquered by nomads.

"O. Bopearachchi, who has produced what probably remains the most reliable Bactrian chronology (see Table I) ... suggests two kings who may have ruled around 185 B.C.E., Demetrius I and Agathocles, as potential founders. Reluctantly, he dismisses the important Menander as too late for this date."[2] However, Jakobsson (2009) believes "the exact date 186/5 B.C.E. may not be so important, and that a later king, such as Menander, may well have been instrumental in the creation of the era."[3]

The expansion of the Greco-Bactrians into northern India from 180 BCE established the Indo-Greek Kingdom, which was a number of various dynastic polities traditionally associated with a number of regional capitals. These dynastic polities were ruled by more than 30 kings, often in conflict with each other. The Greco-Bactrians were originally a Greek colony under the Seleucid Syrian Kingdom of Selecus I. At the beginning of 250 BCE, the Greco-Bactrian state became independent and occupied the former Persian provinces of Bactria and Sogdiana. The expansion of the Greco-Bactrians led to the political dominance of a portion of India by the Greek invaders. Successive nomadic invasions by Scythians and other nomads isolated the Indo-Greeks from the wider Hellenic world after 145 BCE. By the beginning of the first century CE, the Greco-Bactrian state was extinguished as an independent entity. However, the Greek alphabet survived until the Islamic conquest as the script of the Bactrian language, and the conversion of a Indo-Greek King to Buddhism became a part of the zeitgeist of the Indian collective historical memory. [4]

General Description[edit]

The Indo-Greek 'kingdom' was created after the Greco-Bactrians invaded northern India from 180 BCE. It consisted of a number of dynastic polities that ruled from regional capitals and formed a single entity only to the extent their rulers were able to collaborate. More than 30 kings are known, who were often in conflict with each other.[14]

Bopearachchi suggests the period was founded by two kings, Demetrius I and Agathocles, who ruled around 185 BCE, but Jakobsson (2009) believes that a later king known as Menander was "instrumental in the creation of the era."[15] The lack of consistent or reliable sources from either Western or Chinese sources means that much of what we know is speculative and reliant on numismatic evidence.[16]

It is likely the rulers, who simultaneously produced their own coinage, ruled different parts of the Indo-Greek polity and employed their own administrators.[17] Governance of the Indo-Greek region was for the most part through personal kingship and organization extended only to the limits of a particular king's power.[18]

After 145 BCE, Successive nomadic invasions by Scythians and other nomads isolated the Indo-Greeks from the wider Hellenic world. By the beginning of the first century CE, the Greco-Bactrian state was extinguished as an independent entity.[19]

Of the legacy of the civilization, the Greek alphabet survived until the Islamic conquest as the script of the Bactrian language, and the conversion of a Indo-Greek King to Buddhism became a part of the zeitgeist of the Indian collective historical memory.[20]

Central Bureaucracy[edit]

♠ State-level administrators ♣ present ♥

Scholars have inferred that some element of the Seleucid professional bureaucracy was preserved by the Greco-Bactrians.[183][184] The city of Ai Khanoum, of the Greco-Bactrian kingdom, is known to have had "a Persian-style administrative center"[185] so departments and scribes can be inferred from such an apparatus. The Indo-Greek king Demetrius built a new capital city at Sirkap[186] "centered on commerce and ritual".[187] This suggests the ruler must have had access to both architects and vast financial resources that could be spent on a large infrastructure project. This strongly indicates the ruler had access to professional administrators including a tax system. A developed financial administration staffed by professional administrators certainly might explain their efficient coinage system: the "Indo-Greeks were the first rulers to issue coins having the name, title and portrait of the ruler who issued them."[188] The street plan of Sirkap and layout followed traditional Greek urban design[189] which might additionally tell us that the Indo-Greek administrators would have been Greeks (or Indians assimilated to Greek culture). The professional bureaucracy was likely derived from earlier Seleucid and Greek Bactrian models which was overseen by a dioikites.[190] The title dioketes in Seleucid central government denoted the individual responsible for finances, royal land, revenue and expenditure in the satrapies and whom possibly also supervised royal mints and registry offices.[191] Below the dioikites were epistates[192] which, if it is intended to be translated as the usual Ancient Greek term, is any sort of superintendent or overseer. The professional administrators were likely paid by salary (coinage) and/or land grants.[193] Numismatic evidence suggests kingship was collaborative but there are few reliable sources to provide details.[194] It is likely the rulers, who simultaneously produced their own coinage, ruled different parts of the Indo-Greek polity and employed their own administrators. That few of the rulers who succeeded Menander "could easily be named as his relatives, and the Indo-Greek realms were scarcely united after his death"[195] suggests the Indo-Greek region was for the most part not a united state and organization extended only to the limits of a particular king's power. "Hellenistic kingship was personal, not defined by exact borders."[196] In his own realm the king who produced the coinage with his name, title and portrait[197] was basis of the state.

Description of the Normative Ideology

"[T]he descendants of Alexander's settlers, who maintained, to some extent, Greek language and customs in the East, considered themselves to be Greek, and were described as such by their neighbours[...]. The Greekness of Greco-Bactrian and Indo-Greek religion is, however, more difficult to defend. The religious practices of people who asserted, in public discourse, and especially political imagery, a connection with Greeks and Greece were diverse. Greek gods were depicted on coins and temple images, but whether in the use of Near Eastern temple designs or in the dedication of Buddhist relics, Greek cults were only part of the religious constellation of the region.

"[...]

"As well as the cults of Greek gods, other religions flourished in the Greco-Bactrian and Indo-Greek kingdoms. In Bactria, the river Oxus was worshipped, and the archaeological evidence is also suggestive of the existence of other local and Near Eastern cults, both within the precincts of shrines such as the Ai Khanoum temples and the Temple of the Oxus, and in independent locations. In India, we find evidence of the importance of Buddhism and the cults of Indian gods both to the region's inhabitants, Greek and non-Greek, and in political display.

"There was considerable flexibility in iconography and religious practice in the Hellenistic Far East: sites might serve as foci of multiple religious rites, perhaps with different ethnic 'slants', and be patronized by more than one ethnic group, or by individuals with a more complex personal ethnic identity. The use of multiple names for the same temple or divine image need not necessarily have operated at the level of officially orchestrated or approved syncretism. But if this did occur, as I suspect it did, it may have allowed diverse ethnic communities to use the same site without the necessity of 'appropriating' the deity and its worship for any particular ethnic affiliation. This is not to impute any degree of 'split personality' to the identity and iconography of the divinity. Greeks may have looked at the 'Dioskouroi' at Dil'berdzhin and seen one thing, and local Bactrians may have looked at them and seen another (how would a Greek and a Bactrian have referred to them in conversation with one another?). On the other hand, it is possible that people who looked at such an image did so with a conscious awareness that it was more all-encompassing, something that could have the attributes of one god without denying its identity as the other."[300]

Normative Ideological Aspects of Slavery[edit]

♠ Ideology forbids slavery for co-ethnics ♣ inferred absent ♥ “I am not aware of any explicit condemnation of slavery as an institution by any ancient philosopher, nor any movement, large or small, to abolish slavery. Zenon, the founder of Stoicism, owned slaves according to his biography (by Diogenes Laertius). Two anecdotes about Zenon: "They say that he was once scourging a slave whom he had detected in theft; and when he said to him, 'It was fated that I should steal ;' he rejoined, ‘Yes, and that you should be beaten.’ …’On one occasion, when he saw a slave belonging to one of his friends severely bruised, he said to his friend, “I see the footsteps of your anger.”’ The principal Stoic teaching about slavery (notable in Seneca and Musonius Rufus, but surely drawing on earlier teachers) was that masters should not mistreat slaves and should remember that a slave is not an inferior person, only one with a worse fortune. Both Stoics and Epicureans made much of the idea that ‘true slavery’ is not external but an internal matter: whether one is a slave to one’s desires and emotions. Thus every slave can be ‘free’ and every master can be “enslaved” depending on their character. Even Epictetus, who was a slave himself, did not advocate the abolishment of slavery (nor of course did the Christians). It seems to have been unthinkable to them, given how embedded it was in the social and economic structure of their society. Traditional Greco-Roman religion had nothing doctrinal to say about slavery as a social institution, so far as I can tell; in practice it was accepted and not regarded as problematic. It is true that sanctuaries sometimes owned slaves, just as they owned land or other forms of property. During the Hellenistic period, religious devotion to certain deities was conceptualized as a form of willing slavery; this is known in Ptolemaic Egypt, for example. People would ‘enslave’ themselves for a period to gods like Sarapis.” [329]
