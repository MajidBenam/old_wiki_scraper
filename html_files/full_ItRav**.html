General Description[edit]

We begin our Exarchate of Ravenna polity in 568 CE, the date of the last praetorian prefect in Italy, although the first 'exarch' (essentially a governor with political and military authority) known by name dates to the last decades of the 6th century.[13] This year saw Lombard and Germanic tribes invade northern Italy, expelling the Byzantine influence in the area which the Byzantine Emperor Justinian I (r. 527-565 CE) had established only about a decade earlier. The Exarchate of Ravenna together with most of central Italy and parts of the south were the only parts of the former Ostrogothic Kingdom to remain under Byzantine authority after this time. Shortly afterwards, perhaps around 575 CE or slightly later, the first exarch of Ravenna was created under the aegis of the Byzantine emperor.[14] The Exarchate period ended when the last exarch, Eutychius, was killed during the Lombard conquest of the territory in 751 CE. However, recognition of the nominal Byzantine authority in the region persisted until 781 CE, when the years of the emperor's reign were no longer used for dating papal documents or on the coins minted in Rome.[15]

Population and political organization

The Exarchate of Ravenna was essentially a special province of the East Roman Empire and the exarch owed nominal allegiance to the emperor in Constantinople. The Roman Senate was last known operating in 580 CE[16] and many of the senators moved to Constantinople 'to maintain access to court appointments'.[17] In Italy, Ravenna was the undisputed home and capital for the army and civil administration.[18]
The exarch was a ruler who combined civil and military powers,[19] commanding over 50,000 square kilometres of land in Italy after a period of protracted warfare had destroyed the Roman-friendly Ostrogothic governmental system.[20] His duties included leading the army in Italy, publishing and enforcing laws and canons of church councils, and appointing most subordinate officials.[21] Exarchs were appointed by the East Roman Emperor from among the personnel of his palace administration in Constantinople 'and rarely had served in any capacity in Italy before being named exarch'.[22] The criteria for choosing exarchs is not entirely clear, nor is it certain how long they might have expected their official tenure to last.[23] What is clear is that the exarch retained a great deal of authority and autonomy in the region.
Under the exarch, dukes of provinces ruled from cities (e.g. Rome, Naples, Rimini, Venice) and tribunes governed in towns. Like the exarch himself, the role of dukes and tribunes combined both military and civic duties. However, these men, 'along with the lower-level officers and troops, administrators, clerks, and tax collectors were drawn primarily from the local population, including educated laymen, although on occasion an official might be sent from Constantinople'.[24] Ravenna was governed both directly by the exarchal administration[25] and, until the mid-7th century, a city council (curia) responsible for tax collection and certifying and storing legal documents.[26] Decentralization from 600 to 750 CE weakened the authority of the exarch,[27] and as government collapsed, the dukes in the provinces gained power.[28] The 7th century CE saw the development of an 'increasingly closed and hereditary land and officeholding aristocracy'.[29]
Another important rival of the exarch's authority was the archbishop of Ravenna. The exarch worked with the archbishop of Ravenna 'in legal cases, foreign affairs, papal relations, and other similar sorts of situations',[30] but sometimes they clashed on 'doctrinal matters'.[31] The Church also financed public works projects, such as building churches and public baths.[32]
Italy experienced a slight population expansion during this period, and had perhaps over one million inhabitants.[33]

Military Organization[edit]

♠ Standing armies ♣ present ♥ Just prior to this period Emperor Maurice "resorted to the policy of applying the defence system of the frontier zones [castella settlements] to the whole of Italy, threatened as it was by the Lombards. ... The setting up of these catella led to an agrarian revolution in Italy. ... To establish castella and military settlers meant dividing up these large estates. The method adopted was expropriation without compensation, but the soldiers stationed in the castellum were granted land in the form of an emphyteunsis, that is, hereditary lease. The actual ownership of the land remained with the proprietors and the soldiers had to pay them a fixed annual rent in return. This relationship with the landowners meant that the garrison of the castellum did not form an independent community, for the castellum was established on private property. ... The development of this relationship between the soldiers and the landed proprietors, based on leasehold, was of extreme importance for the West. Under Germanic influence the nature of the emphyteusis began to change and what had been a Roman legal concept now turned into a relationship of vassalage. The relation between soldier and landed proprietor, which from the Roman point of view was regarded as a private contract, began to be interpreted as a relation between lord and vassal. ... the two largest landlords in imperial Italy, the Pope and the archbishop of Ravenna, now became territorial rulers. In the case of the papacy this situtation was given legal validity by the Franks, so that the Pope was at least de jure ruler of the land of which he had formerly been proprietor." [94]

♠ Name ♣ baptism ♥ Baptism was the fundamental rite of initiation into Christianity going back to Augustine's time, but changed substantially during the early medieval period. Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ mass ♥ The Mass was the fundamental ritual of Christianity; indeed, as Arnold as argued, "...in whatever form, the mass can be seen as a key ritual in the production of medieval community...."[167] There were many varieties of masses, varying in frequency and nature depending on the community involved. Monasteries and convents held several masses a day, in particular masses for the dead.[168] Parish priests were supposed to say one mass daily, but it is unclear whether this was common, especially during this period, which predates the general establishment of parish churches in localities. High mass was rare, most likely restricted to high holidays such as Christmas; the laity were only expected to attend mass on Sundays, and even this was not asserted as a fundamental part of orthodox Christianity until a later period.[169] A rough description of a typical mass is as follows. The priest read out a psalm on the nature of original sin, followed by hymns on Sundays. Then the priest read from the Gospel, and prepared the bread and wine for the Eucharist (at this point, the laity would give any offerings they had for the collection box). This description is from (Arnold)[170] [171] The priest then passed the Pax, an object with the crucifix on it, around and the laity kissed it.[172] This seems to have taken the place of Communion on days when the laity did not participate in it; mass then ended. Monastic masses and special liturgical masses would have been slightly different, but these were the main elements. NOTE: Most Christians-in fact, probably the vast majority, during this period-did not attend mass on anything approaching a regular basis, perhaps at all.[173] Urban people, in particular those in Rome, probably attended church more regularly than, say, those in the remote Appenines, but this is no more than a reasonable hypothesis for this period. I would also like to note the serious paucity of sources, and thus of secondary scholarship, on the early medieval mass in Italy: we simply do not know very much about lived religion in early medieval Europe, and especially in Italy.

"The Byzantine province of Italy consisted of the Exarchate of Ravenna and several duchies: Istria, Venetia, Ferrara, the Pentapolis, Rome, Perugia, Naples, and Calabria. "The head of the Byzantine administration was the exarch, always sent from Constantinople, who might be a military official like Isaac, a eunuch bureaucrat like Eleutherius, Olympius, and Eutychius, or some other government functionary. The curia disappears from view after the mid-seventh century, but we do find in Ravenna men with the title iudex, which literally means 'judge' but in the seventh and eighth centuries was used to designate any sort of government official, or indeed any members of the leading class of landowners."[300]

Government "was composed of both local and foreign officials who performed a variety of functions.
