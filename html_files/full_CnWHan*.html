General Description[edit]

The Western Han dynasty (also known as the Former Han) was the first lasting imperial dynasty in China.[18] In 206 BCE, the first imperial Han emperor Liu Bang defeated the Qin and capture the capital of Xianyang, but was forced to yield to the rival Western Chu state.[19] A period of conflict between Chu and Han lasted until 202 BCE, when Liu Bang defeated the Western Chu and declared himself emperor of the Han dynasty. (San 68) He was the first commoner to become the emperor of China.[20]

The seventh emperor of Han, Emperor Wu (r. 141-87 BCE), expanded the Western Han territory to modern Xinjiang and south China.[21] During Wu Di’s rule Western Han dynasty encompassed modern China, northern Vietnam, Inner Mongolia, southern Manchuria, and parts of modern Korea.[22]

The Western Han dynasty is known for its economic, technological, and artistic innovations. The opening of the Silk Road in 130 BCE linked China to Central Asia, Africa, Europe, and the Middle East.[23] The state controlled the production of salt, iron, and coins, and developed waterways and irrigation.[24] The use of the iron plough and other iron agricultural tools became widespread.[25] Han artisans developed new techniques for metalwork, spinning, weaving, wood carving and pottery.[26]

The Western Han were overthrown by Wang Mang, who ruled as the emperor of the Xin dynasty from 9-23 CE.[27]

Population and political organization

The Western Han dynasty was marked by a strong imperial government and a combination of centrally-controlled commandaries and semi-autonomous kingdoms.[28] The central government promoted Confucianism as a state doctrine.[29] The Western Han gradually reduced the size of the semi-autonomous kingdoms within the empire. Many kings and marquises were eventually replaced by members of the imperial clan.[30] Commanderies were ruled a civil governor and military governor and were divided into counties or districts.[31]

An imperial academy was established in 124 BCE. Qualification through Confucian examinations slowly replaced hereditary assignment of government positions.[32] Although exams were used only sporadically due to the significantly aristocratic society of this period. [33]


The population of the Western Han empire was 57.6 million in 2 CE[34], and 60 million at its peak.[35] The Western Han capital of Chang’an was home to between 250,000 and 400,000 people. [36][37]

Description of the Ritual System

In general, ritual system seems to have remained largely in-tact in the region from the Western Zhou period, with some additions during the imperial period relating to the activities of the early Emperors (esp. the feng ceremony)

Puett 2014a: “Humans were seen to be composed of numerous different energies, souls, and powers. Some of these energies (qi) were associated with the emotions of the human, while the souls (the hun and po) were associated with the personality. Another component was the spirit (shen), which is what gave humans consciousness, as well as the ability to control things. While alive, humans would try to cultivate these energies and their spirit, trying to refine them and to keep them within the human body…. In short, if the ghosts of recently deceased humans were dangerous in the sense of being potentially angry and resentful against the living, the spirits (both the spirits of humans who died long ago, as well as the other spirits in the human) tended to be dangerous in another sense - highly powerful, but relatively indifferent to the needs of humans. The ghosts were potentially malicious toward the living, and the spirits were (from the point of view of the living humans) indifferent and potentially capricious….This is the background against which to understand early Chinese religious practices. The problem was clear: humans had to deal with dangerous recently deceased ghosts, as well as powerful spirits who tended to be unpredictable. The goal of early Chinese practices was to transform these figures as much as possible - or at least forge relationships with them - such that they would become more responsive to the needs of the living….For the recently deceased, the first concern was to separate the personality of the deceased from the spirit. Ritual actions would be undertaken to keep the souls of the deceased with the body, which would be buried in a tomb. The goal would be to keep the souls in the tomb, removed from the living. This would be accomplished in part through ritual exhortations that the souls not leave the tomb. It would also be accomplished by making the tombs places where the souls would want to remain. 13 bureaus: #West Bureau (oversaw appointment of lower ranking officials); #East Bureau (in charge of promotion or dismissal of lower ranking officials); #The Bureau of Imperial Household (in charge of revenues of the imperial household and supervising sacrifice to the imperial ancestors); #The Bureau of Memorials (responsible for all the memorials presented to the emperor); #The Bureau of criminal law; #The Bureau of Standards and postal service; #The Bureau of Military Transportation (taking charge of troop transportation and managing various forms of transportations and logistics); #The Bureau of Bandit Control; #The Bureau of Criminal Executions; #The Bureau of Soldiers; #The Bureau of Gold (issuing currency and operating monopolized state industries such as iron and salt production); #The Bureau of Granaries (taking charge of levies, taxes, and the storage of state grain); #The Bureau of Cabinet (maintenance of all government records and supervision of the works of all other bureaus).

♠ Charity ♣ present ♥

acts of genuine charity seem restricted to imperial largesse, including bestowals of rank (which carried material and social benefits), pardons of crimes and obligations, tax remittance, gifts of money especially to people who suffered some disaster, sacrifices and distributions of food, land grants, and goods for aged and disabled people. Public building and infrastructure development do not seem to have been a focus of imperial largesse. [760]

As for non-imperial charity, historical sources, especially the Shiji, complained that 'charitable' acts of wealthy merchants were undermining the state's authority by fostering loyalty and obligations from the recipients to the merchants.[761]

Further, wealthy non-state local elite, particularly merchants, noted as engaging in frequent acts of charity as a means of culling favor from the poorer members of society, and perhaps acting as a parallel or competitive line of authority in opposition to the central state.[762]

Lewis 2014, however, notes that in general the Han state took great care to assert their own authority in most public goods, including construction, standardized weights and measures, regulating market space and activity, etc.[763] Thus, the acts of civic benefaction by wealthy, non-state elite seem restricted to particular localities and likely exaggerated in the accounts of Sima Qian in the Shiji.

Buddhist monasteries received land grants from the state and aristocrats and population tied to the land. [764]
