General Description[edit]

The eleventh century BCE marks the beginning of radical changes in southern Greece generally as well as Crete specifically, largely resulting from the invasion from the North of the Dorians[1] Overall, however, this period is relatively poorly understood, with no written sources and few archaeological finds. Most likely, Cretans mainly dedicated themselves to farming and pastoralism. Writing disappeared and artistic expression became more abstracted and geometrical. Things started to change in the eighth century, when trade routes were revitalized, and Cretans were able to capitalize on the island's premier location in the Eastern Mediterranean. And the trade in artefacts and products was accompanied by the exchange of new ideas and technologies. [2]

Population and political organization

Not much is known about either the island's population numbers at the time, or its political organization. In terms of population, very few settlements have been excavated, and none of these have yielded enough data for a credible estimate; in terms of political organization, it is likely that elite families were in charge but not much else could be said.[3]

Ritual variables[edit]

♠ RA ♣ Enrico Cioni ♥ KC carried out the original coding. EC edited some sections and re-coded others based on suggestions provided by Eleni Hatzaki and Colin MacDonald at the May 2016 conference in Knossos.

♠ Expert ♣ Kostis Christakis ♥

In the early 1st millennium BCE, after the advent of the Dorians, the Twelve Gods of Olympus, were introduced to Crete and become the official region of the local communities as it was for those in the rest of the Greek World.[30] References to Linear B tablets indicates that many of these gods were already worshiped during the Mycenaean period. The official religion was enriched with elements of the primeval Minoan religious tradition, creating a unique amalgam of religious beliefs and ritual. The most significant example of this intermingling of Minoan and Greek religious beliefs is that of Zeus. According to Hesiod’s version written in the 8th century BCE, Zeus was born and nursed in Crete (the Idaean cave is the most likely candidate for the birth and raising place of the god). Zeus cult in Crete had a mystic character, which was totally foreign to the cult of the god in the rest of the Greek World. The Cretan Zeus is the Minoan youthful god of fertility, son and husband of the Great Mother Goddess, who represent the growth, decay and renewal of the nature; hence the Cretan Zeus annually died and reborn. Thus the Zeus in Crete was different from the Greek Zeus, the sky-god and Lord of men and the world, and legend about his death and tomb was created, lasting until relatively recent times. Early in the Iron Age (11th-8th centuries BCE) cult was usually practiced in small house-shrines located within the settlement like those at Karphi (12-10th centuries BCE), Vrokastro (12th-8th century BCE), and Kavousi (10th-8th centuries BCE). The earliest free-standing temple in Crete -if not the earliest sanctuary in the Greek area- was excavated at Kommos (south-central Crete). The temple, a small rectangular shine, was constructed around 1025 BCE. After two centuries of intermittent use, perhaps by passers-by and seamen, Kommos was a convenient stopping point - the first temple was succeeded by two others successively built temples (9th - 4th centuries BCE). Phoenician amphorae, figurines and three stone pillars set into a base block, the Phoenician representation of a triad of gods, indicates the close contacts between Crete and Phoenicia in the late 9th and the 8th centuries BCE. Another important sanctuary was that of Dreros built in the Agora of the mountain city in east Crete. This one-room temple was provided with a central hearth. Three small statues identified as the gods Apollo and Artemis and their mother Leto stood on top of a stone structure which contained goat horns and bronze objects and may have served as an altar for animal sacrifices. They are the earliest known Greek hammered bronze statues (mid 8th century BCE) combining features from the Geometric and Daedalic style. Free-standing sanctuaries were also found at Palaiakstro (9th - 6th centuries BCE) and Praisos (8th - 6th centuries BCE) in east Crete both dedicated to Zeus, at Smari (8th - 7th centuries BCE) and Gortys (7th century BCE) in Central Crete dedicated to the goddess Athena and at Prinias and Afrati also in Central Crete (7th century BCE). The temple at Prinias is decorated with monumental sculptures including a frieze with a ritual procession of horsemen. Cult ritual continued inside caves, a typical Cretan form of cult dated from Prehistory and continued to this date. The most famous and important cult cave was the Idaean on the Mount Ida, birth and upbringing place of Zeus. It was a centre of initiation and purification rites. Ritual activity, attested from the the 2nd millennium BCE to Roman times (4th cent. AD), peaked during the Geometric and Archaic periods (8th - 7th centuries BCE). Another cave also associated with Zeus is the Phychro cave in East Crete (8th - 7th centuries BCE). The cave of Eileithyia at Inatos (north-central Crete) was dedicated to Eileithyia, protectress of childbirth. Data points to rituals involving the offering of figurines of couples and pregnant women, representations of childbirth and nursing mothers. Votive offerings include terra-cotta and bronze human and animal figurines, relief mould-made plaques, vessels, and precious utensils, bronze tripods, cauldrons and shields -the fineast examples products of high craftmanship were found in the Idaean Cave- and weapons. Bronze cut-out sheets decorated by hammering and engraving offered to the Sanctuary of Syme by individual votaries provide crucial information on religious ideology and ritual. The depiction of the god Hermes straddling a tree branch allude to the Bronze Age cult ritual of the Sacred Tree, interweaving the Minoan with the Dorian religious ideology. Many others depict hunters carrying animals or their dismembered quarry for sacrifice alluding to initiation rites and the education of the young.

Rituals of initiation were very important in the Doric Crete. An inscribed marble stele from the sanctuary of Zeus at Palaikastro record the Hymn of Kouretes addressed to Zeus in order to ensure peace, fertility, happiness, good journeys for ships, good government for cities and protection for young. It was sung by young men and was related with related to rites of passage from adolescence to adulthood. The annual ceremony included circular dances with music around the god’s altar. The text was inscribed in the 3rd century but it was originally composited to late 4th or early 3rd century BCE. It echoes, however, earlier religious beliefs. The most important ritual of initiation was that involving the kidnapping of a boy from a grown-up men. The ritual was described by Ephorus of Cyme in Strabo’s Geography: [31]"[The Cretans] have a peculiar custom in regard to love affairs, for they win the objects with their love, not by persuasion, but by abduction; the lover tells the friends of the boy three or four days beforehand that he is going to make the abduction; but for the friends to conceal the boy, or not to let him go forth the appointed road, is indeed a most disgraceful thing, a confession, as it were, that the boy is unworthy to obtain such a lover; and when they meet, if the abductor is the boy’s equal or superior in rank or other respects, the friends pursue him and lay hold of him, though only in a very gentle way, thus satisfying the custom; and after that they cheerfully turn the boy over to him to lead away; if, however, the abductor is unworthy, they take the boy away from him." "It is disgraceful for those who are handsome in appearance or descendants of illustrious ancestors to fail to obtain lovers, the presumption being that their character (masculinity) is responsible for such a fate. But the parastathentes (those who stand by their lover in battle) receive honors; for in both the dances and the races they have the positions of highest honor, and are allowed to dress in better clothes than the rest, that is, in the habit given them by their lovers; and not then only, but even after they have grown to manhood, they wear a distinctive dress, which is intended to make known the fact that each wearer has become kleinos, for they call the loved one kleinos (distinguished) and the lover philter." “The youth sacrifices the ox to Zeus and gives a feast to those who came down with him from the mountains. He then declares, concerning his relationship with the lover, whether it took place with his consent or not; the convention encourages this in order that, if any violence is used against him in the abduction, he may restore his honor and break off the relationship.” The function of this initiatory homosexuality was to teach the youth and initiate him in the customs of adults and to “offer both lover and beloved the chance to give proof of a noble character deserving of respect”. [32]

♠ Name ♣Ekdysia or Periblemata♥ Ekdysia or Periblemata is essentially a single ritual. [87] The ceremony combined elements of fertility, initiation and marriage-ritual and clearly have roots in the earliest stratum of Cretan religion. For the youth were apparently initiated into manhood, citizenship and marriage at the same period of life. When growth was achieved, the boy died as a boy and was born again as a man, cast aside his boyhood costume and wore mans clothes. The ritual, took place during the spring, culminated in a festival in which the young men put of the garments of ephebic age (Ekdysia) and received the garment of the citizen (Periblemata). The ritual was followed by solemn oaths, promising to protect their city, defend their allies and fight agains their enemies, feasts, and collective marriages. The organization of these rites was on the responsibility of the state institutions. It is very like that these rites goes back to the Bronze Age period.[88]

Deification of Rulers[edit]

♠ Rulers are legitimated by gods ♣ inferred present ♥ Use of expensive, conspicuous items such as bronze shields and tripods as votive offerings at important sanctuaries suggests that elites intended to gain legitimacy by signalling a special relation with the gods. "The military-aristocratic connotations of the dedicated shields speak for themselves. The possession of this kind of armor, often elaborately decorated, was clearly the reserve of an elite. Bronze shields and other weaponry occur as grave offerings in rich Cretan tombs from the late ninth to the seventh century bc and would have articulated the deceased’s role as warrior and leading member of the developing aristocracy. The dedication of such objects in sanctuaries would have had a similar function, but added an element of greater visibility and permanency. Precious votives could remain on display for generations, as attested by ancient authors such as Pausanias. He mentions seeing such votives and being told that these were of great antiquity and offered by famous rulers (e.g. Paus, III.2.8, III.3.8.,V.19.6). Clearly, the religious dedication of precious objects implied special relations with higher powers. Like a claim to military prowess, this relationship enhanced a donor’s position in the community and would have been considered as more than worth the expense.//The bronze tripod-cauldrons, though lacking the overt military connotations of the shields, were no less tied to the aristocratic ethos. An indication of their value may be gained from the Homeric epics, in which they constitute the most prestigious gift exchanged between fellow aristocrats. In a passage of the Iliad (23.703-5), a tripod is estimated to have a value of twelve oxen, whereas a skilled, female slave is considered to be worth only four. O. Murray (1983) has argued that the special meaning of bronze tripod-cauldrons in early Greek times derived from their use as cooking vessels at communal dining parties. Drawing on anthropological parallels and the Homeric epics, Murray describes these as competitive, ritualized affairs, in which the leading members of society would try to outdo each other in generosity, including the provision of vast quantities of food. Tripod-cauldrons would thus have developed into symbols of the owner’s wealth and - no less importantly - of the ability to feed, sustain and organize a group of followers. As pointed out by W. Burkert, bronze cauldrons were also the ideal vessel in which to boil the parts of the sacrificial meat that were not roasted. Their frequent setting up in sanctuaries may have been a way of “giving permanence to the sacrificial act” (Burkert 1985:93)." [143]

♠ Human sacrifice ♣ [2-3] ♥ Inferred present due to a find at Eleutherna, although the date could place this event either in the Geometric or the Archaic period. "The display ends with Funerary Pyre, the unique funerary pyre of a young aristocrat warrior, aged approximately 30 years, who was cremated with his companion in c. 720-700 BC. This pyre contained a unique find at its northwest edge: the remains of a well-built man 30-40 years old. The body’s position at the edge and clearly outside of the pyre’s limits, its unnatural crescent-like posture, the fact that it was headless, unburnt, and essentially without grave offerings suggest that it belonged to a man who did not die of natural causes. It is difficult to say whether this was an ordinary execution, ritual revenge, or a propitiatory sacrifice. The most widely accepted hypothesis is that the man was a prisoner-of-war who was executed in front of the warrior’s pyre. Based on this unique discovery and on the Homeric description of a similar event (Iliad, Book XXIII, 110-179 ff.), Professor N. Chr. Stampolidis was able to reconstruct the funerary ritual with his collaborators and students in 1996. Thus, the core of the saga of Patroclus’s funerary pyre, with its Cretan protagonists Idomeneus and Meriones, appears to have Cretan origins. The importance of the Orthi Petra necropolis lies not only in the fact that it provides the tangible remains of a Homeric burial practice, but also in that it forms a typical illustration of the verses of the Iliad and the Odyssey relating to a specific ritual." ♠ Omniscient supernatural beings ♣ absent ♥ “Everything depends on what you mean by “omniscience.” First, there is the distinction between “knowing everything important,” like who committed a crime or who will win the war, and true omniscience, which includes everything which can be known (things like “the fall of a sparrow” and how many grains of sand are on a beach). Very few Greek gods claimed true omniscience. The only example I know is that it was claimed for Apollo at Delphi, who was specifically an oracular god. So it was his business to know everything. The claim seems to have been put forward by the sanctuary in order to enhance his prestige. Greek philosophers presumably believed in divine omniscience because they represented the gods or the divine as perfect, which would require absolute knowledge, but these issues do not seem to have really come to the fore in the literature until the Roman period and Christianity, so far as I can tell. Even if omniscience only means “knowing everything that it is important to know,” I have my doubts about whether the Greek gods possessed it. I would say that it was always contextual. They knew whatever it was important to know in a given context. Greeks did not necessarily assume that the gods could read their minds and knew the inner secrets of their heart and so forth. However, they definitely assumed that gods could see and hear everything they did. I don’t think the gods were concerned with people’s thoughts unless these thoughts manifested themselves in words or actions. This is not to say that they could never read minds, just that it wasn’t part and parcel of how they were represented, the way it is, for example, with the Christian god.” [166]

"The military-aristocratic connotations of the dedicated shields speak for themselves. The possession of this kind of armor, often elaborately decorated, was clearly the reserve of an elite. Bronze shields and other weaponry occur as grave offerings in rich Cretan tombs from the late ninth to the seventh century bc and would have articulated the deceased’s role as warrior and leading member of the developing aristocracy. The dedication of such objects in sanctuaries would have had a similar function, but added an element of greater visibility and permanency. Precious votives could remain on display for generations, as attested by ancient authors such as Pausanias. He mentions seeing such votives and being told that these were of great antiquity and offered by famous rulers (e.g. Paus, III.2.8, III.3.8.,V.19.6). Clearly, the religious dedication of precious objects implied special relations with higher powers. Like a claim to military prowess, this relationship enhanced a donor’s position in the community and would have been considered as more than worth the expense.//The bronze tripod-cauldrons, though lacking the overt military connotations of the shields, were no less tied to the aristocratic ethos. An indication of their value may be gained from the Homeric epics, in which they constitute the most prestigious gift exchanged between fellow aristocrats. In a passage of the Iliad (23.703-5), a tripod is estimated to have a value of twelve oxen, whereas a skilled, female slave is considered to be worth only four. O. Murray (1983) has argued that the special meaning of bronze tripod-cauldrons in early Greek times derived from their use as cooking vessels at communal dining parties. Drawing on anthropological parallels and the Homeric epics, Murray describes these as competitive, ritualized affairs, in which the leading members of society would try to outdo each other in generosity, including the provision of vast quantities of food. Tripod-cauldrons would thus have developed into symbols of the owner’s wealth and - no less importantly - of the ability to feed, sustain and organize a group of followers. As pointed out by W. Burkert, bronze cauldrons were also the ideal vessel in which to boil the parts of the sacrificial meat that were not roasted.
