General Description[edit]

Mataram started out as a vassal to the kingdom of Pajang—itself one of a number of short-lived polities that emerged from the disintegration of the Demak Sultanate—and gradually established itself as the dominant polity in central Java between the end of the sixteenth and the beginning of the seventeenth centuries.[5] The polity's heyday coincided with the rule of Agung Hanyokrokusumo (1613-1645), whose marriage alliances and military campaigns resulted in the polity's greatest territorial expansion, annexing the Sultanate of Cirebon in the West and the kingdoms of Surabaya and Blambangan in the East.[6] Mataram went in decline shortly after Hanyokrokusumo's death, succumbing to the Dutch East India Company in the first half of the eighteenth century.[7]

Population and political organization

The Sultan governed with the assistance of a number of functionaries, though the exact hierarchy of these functionaries remains unclear, as does their relationship to the bureaucratic systems in the polity's administrative subdivisions, particularly its powerful trading centers on the coast.[8] It is worth noting, however, that Hanyokrokusumo enacted a sweeping reform of the judiciary system meant to integrate Islamic law into traditional customs.[9]
No demographic estimates have been found in the specialist literature, with the exception of Reid's[10] conjecture that the polity's population density corresponded to about thirty people per squared kilometer.

Information on the administration of the Mataram Sultanate is very scarce. It seems that the Majapahit structure of the ruler and a few top functionaries with varying influence was retained. There were various different titles for functionaries, but it is unclear whether there was a particular hierarchical structure between them, and moreover the relationship of these functionaries to powerful coastal regions, and the administrative structures within these regions, is far from clear and there was probably much fluidity and development over time.[15] Moertono shows that in later Mataram (possibly after the VOC came to dominate) there was a ligion including rendering justice in disputes under the jurisdiction of Islamic law. Thseparate and more independent department, the reh pangulon, which was responsible for matters of ree institution of the penggulu (head of the clergy in the main mosque in the king's capital) was gradually incorporated into the administrative system as head of a special division. The penggulu had his say about appointing lower penggulu naibs, who each administered the religious affairs of a certain number of villages. These lower officials were not thought of as belonging to the king's administration, for unlike other royal officials they did not receive income from the king.[16]

Most widespread collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ slametan ♥ "A slametan ceremony is usually held in the home of a particular family, and attended by the male members of the household, a number of invited guests (predominantly male), usually the next-door neighbours and acquaintances who live nearby, relatives who live in the same village or town, and a number of very close friends who may or may not live nearby. All these are often invited at short notice. "The ceremony is usually held in the evening in the front room of the house. [...] The food consists of rice cones which are about fifteen in inches high, and which are elaborately decorated with side dishes of fish, meat, eggs, vegetables, and fruits, and which show up the variety of colours and particular shapes of the side dishes (tumpeng). There are also one or two kettles containing tea, a number of empty glasses, bowls containing water for the guests to wash their hands, and a pile of banana leaves and plates. "By the time the guests (usually exclusively male) arrive, Javanese incense (menyana, or dupa) will have been burnt; when everybody is seated on the mats around the rice cone trays, the slametan ceremony is started with a brief address by the host, delivered in formal krami style. He expresses his gratitude for the attendance of the guests, explains the purpose of the gathering, apologizes for all the shortcomings of he arrangements, the organization of the ceremony, and the inadequate food served, and finally requests a religious official (modin or kaum) who is specially invited for the occasion, to chant the incantatios (ndonga), which consist of one or two verses (ayat) from the Qur'an. The presence of a religious official is not needed if, among those who are present, one person who has learned to read the Qur'an can lead the ceremony. This person would have been requested by the host in advance to perform the ndonga. "When the modin starts his ndonga chant, the guests remain in the sila sitting position but with their hands on their knees with the palms turned upward, their faces slightly lifted, and their eyes closed. Occasionally they accentuate the chant of the modin by uttering amin in chorus. "When the ndonga is finished, the modin is invited by the host to begin the meal, and this is followed by the other guests. [...] "In village slametan, the ceremony is often extended by dikir, a monotonous chant of the phrase la'illaha illallah (there is no God except God), repeated in chorus by all participants--a chant which may last for more than one hour at a stretch. The guests will be invited to eat after the dikir. Dikir, however, is usually omitted or drastically reduced to only one-quarter of an hour in the homes of priyayi families in the towns and cities, except for mortuary slametan." [160]
