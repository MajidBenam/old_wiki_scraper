General Description[edit]

Hawai'i, also known as the Big Island, is the largest island of the Hawaiian archipelago. Our 'Hawaii 2' refers to the period from 1200 to 1580 CE. 1200 marks the beginning of archaeologist Patrick Kirch's 'expansion period',[6] while 1580 is the approximate date of the formation of the first island-wide unitary kingdom.[7]

Population and political organization

According to reconstructions of Hawaiki, the ancestral Polynesian homeland, ancient Polynesians recognized the authority of the *ariki, that is, the head of a lineage, who had both secular and sacred authority and was in charge of most, if not all, rituals.[8] However, a few thousand years separate Ancestral Polynesians from the earliest Hawaiians, and it is not clear how much the latter retained of the former's culture and sociopolitical organization. The earliest island-wide unitary kingdom on the Big Island emerged around 1580;[9] before then, the Big Island was probably divided into several small, independent polities.[10]
It is currently not possible to reconstruct the exact population of a typical Big Island community at this time.[11] No up-to-date estimates have been found in the literature. Scholars do, however, distinguish between distinct phases of demographic and agricultural development after the initial colonization period. From 1200 to 1400 CE, Hawaiians experienced significant population growth and adapted their technology and subsistence economy to local conditions while maintaining long-distance contact with Eastern Polynesia. From 1400 to 1580 CE, population growth peaked and began to stabilize, contact with Eastern Polynesia ceased, and large-scale dryland field systems were established across the Big Island.[12]

Hōnaunau was a royal center (for King ‘Umi) after c. 1570-1590CE. It contained various royal and chiefly residences, a pu’uhonua (sanctuary), large temples, and the “Great Wall”[32]. One of the temples, ‘Āle’ale’a has an area of 702 m^2 and volume of 1684m^3; its construction was stared as early as 1250CE[33]. Another of the temples has an area of 2000 m^2[34]. The “Great Wall” at Hōnaunau was probably built around 1600 CE[35]; it has an average height of 3.7 m, width of 5.2 meters, and length of 304.8 meters[36], for a total volume 5,864 m^3. This is the largest construction I have found in the literature, so I put its dimensions in the height/extent variables below.

Lapakahi also had a “Great Wall”, which was built between about 1450 and 1500 CE[37].

At Ahuo-‘Umi, there was a substantial temple on the interior slope of Mt. Hualālai[38].

Fish ponds were probably first built in the fourteenth century[39], but it is not clear if this refers to the Big Island or to elsewhere in the archipelago.

During Liloa’s reign (c. 1550-1570CE), his royal center at Waipi’o Valley had a royal residence (called Kahaunokama’ahala), two large luakini, large fishponds, and a pu’uhonua (place of refuge for kapu-breakers); after his death it also included his mausoleum (Hale o Liloa)[40].

Taxation[edit]

Based on linguistic studies, it seems likely that Ancestral Polynesians (including the earliest inhabitants of Hawai'i) would have celebrated a first fruits or harvest festival: 'Given the common associations between "share," "feast," and more than one reference to first fruits, we suggest that, in PPN, *qinati was a share or portion accompanying a first fruits feast'.[309] In Irving Goldman's model of Polynesian cultural evolution, this early first-fruits institution entailed a 'freely given religious offering' from commoners to chiefs to whom they were related 'as kinsfolk'.[310] During the period of this polity, however, a more formal tax collection system emerged, centred around the annual Makahiki celebrations [311] - I have dated the transition here to the beginning of the 16th century, based on Kirch's descriptions in A Shark Going Inland Is My Chief.[312]
