General Description[edit]

The Judea (originally Yehuda) polity of 141 - 63 BCE was formed when Simon Thassi, brother of the executed Jonathan Apphus who had waged war against the Seleucids, was elected as High King and Prince in a popular assembly in 141 BCE. Simon achieved a measure of quasi-independence from the Seleucids—though he remained a vassal and the population retained strong elements of Hellenism.

According to archaeologists, it seems that Galilee was only sparsely settled before this period, and that the conquering of territories and increase in Jewish communities coming into the area contributed to the rise in permanent settlements and population during this time. However, agreements over the population differs widely, with estimates on the largest settlement, Jerusalem, ranging from 10,000 to 100,000 people.[3][4][5]

Judea was a sophisticated, well-organised and equipped society, with markets, established trade routes, water transportation infrastructure, aqueducts and cisterns, temples and palaces, sporting arenas, libraries, and many other modern features.[6] [7] [8]

The most impressive/costly building(s)[edit]

The Hasmonean palace in Jerusalem itself, which could be assumed to be the most impressive building, has not been found or excavated. However, the winter-palace complex near Jericho has been partly excavated,[18] and was a remarkable achievement for its time; notably, it featured several large swimming pools more than 3 meters deep, filled with water conveyed by conduit from springs to the north, at a time when swimming pools were extremely rare. The complex also featured a large formal garden and paved plazas. During the reign of Alexander Yannai, the palace at the center of the complex was built over with a "fortified palace," defended with thick walls and tall towers; it would have been meant only to hold off a minor disturbance until troops from the nearby fortresses could be summoned. During the reign of Alexandra Salome, the "Twin Palaces" were built south of the pools, perhaps to accommodate each of her sons Yochanan Hyrcanus II and Aristobulus II (who would later fight a series of wars with each other).

Measurement System[edit]

The use of measurement systems in Judea at this time seems to have been eclectic. The ancient Israelite measurements such as sheqels, amot, and se'ot were still in use in religious contexts, but it is unclear whether they were used in daily life. In commercial matters, Greek measurements seem to have been common, and perhaps even predominated—not surprising, given the hundreds of years of Hellenistic control before the Hasmonean revolt.

♠ Length ♣ present ♥ For example, the Books of Maccabees often reference Greek units of measurement such as stadia and schoinoi (e.g. 2 Maccabees 11:5, 12:9); however, it is unknown whether this was an artifact of the Greek translation, and if the original Hebrew used other units. The use of "cubits" and "handbreadths" is attested to as far as the Biblical era, as well as other measurements taken from Egypt and Mesopotamia.
♠ Area ♣ inferred present ♥ The Mishna often uses area measures that describe the amount of land necessary to produce, for example, a se'ah of grain ("beit se'ah"), a practice which dates back to Mesopotamia.
♠ Volume ♣ present ♥ The use of standardized units such as se'ah is attested to back into the Iron Age.[21]
♠ Weight ♣ present ♥ A mix of units from surrounding cultures, such as the sheqel, mina, drachma, etc.
♠ Time ♣ inferred present ♥ Standardized calendars; the sundial and water clock had been extant in the region for centuries by this point.
♠ Geometrical ♣ inferred present ♥ The Hasmoneans lived hundreds of years after Pythagoras and Euclid, and Judea bordered the celebrated Phoenician city-states (known for their mathematicians). A Hebrew mathematical text, משנת המדות (Mishnat haMiddot, "Review of the Measurements"), is typically dated to about 150 C.E. or some two centuries after our period (though some scholars such as Sarfatti argued that it is many centuries older);[22] it is likely that given the heavy influence of Hellenic civilization and the construction of complex siege engines (see below) that geometry was known at least to some degree during the Hasmonean period.

Armor[edit]

Direct evidence for the armor used by the Hasmonean armies is thin: "The main frustration is, of course, that during the Second Temple period, especially between the Maccabean uprising and the destruction of the Second Temple, Jews upheld the Second Commandment—the prohibition on making any graven images—quite strictly. For this reason there is no figurative Jewish art from this period; although the use of symbols was widespread, and pictures of plants and even animals can often be found, human figures are not depicted."[37] The main types of evidence are thus the scanty literary references in Josephus and others, inference from the armor in use during previous eras, and comparison with neighboring Hellenic powers.
