Religion

♠ Religious Tradition ♣ Christianity ♥
♠ Religion-Family ♣ Catholicism ♥
♠ Religion-Sect ♣ Roman Catholic ♥

Christianity had originally been introduced during the Roman occupation of Britain, but with the departure of the Romans in 410 CE and the arrival of the Anglo-Saxons, Celtic and Pagan religions dominated the indigenous population. Christianity was reintroduced to the Anglo-Saxon population of England by Augustine in 597 CE, who was sent to the Kingdom of Kent by Pope Gregory to spread the Christian gospel. Ethelbert, the King of Kent, had married a princess from Gaul who was Christian and so this was deemed a strong starting point. Both Ethelbert and his nephew, who was the King of Essex, converted to Christianity. This trend then began to trickle down through the population and across kingdoms. However, Christianity was not the only, or indeed steady, religion across England, and areas often reverted back to Paganism dependant on the ruler or local populace. However, by around 663 CE the south of England at least were officially Christian kingdoms and it continued to flourish across England throughout the period. Christianity became popular among the kings of England because of the social hierarchy it promoted with kings being below only one god.[5]


♠ Religious Tradition ♣ Anglo-Saxon Paganism ♥
♠ Religion-Family ♣ Paganism ♥

The Germanic paganism that the Anglo-Saxons brought to England with them was based in animism and magic. They worshipped nature. Their priests were believed to possess magical powers. They worshipped many gods including Woden, Frig, Thunor and Tiw. Their religion was similar to that of Norse Paganism.[6]

General description[edit]

The quasi-polity of Anglo-Saxon England existed between the fall of Roman Britain in 410 CE - and the subsequent mass migration into the region of the Germanic speaking Angle, Saxon, and Jute tribes from western Europe - until the unification of the Kingdom of England in 927 CE.

The Saxon and Angle tribes from the Danish peninsula and the North Sea coast had been raiding British shores and occasionally employed as mercenary soldiers as early as 287 CE. But the eventual colonisation of Britain by the Anglo-Saxons effectively wiped out Roman-Briton and Celtic culture, which was replaced by Germanic language, laws and customs. Only in westerly Wales, Scotland and Ireland did the Celtic people continue some form of pre-Anglo-Saxon life. Around 650 CE the separate English kingdoms, forged by the arrival and colonisation of the Anglo-Saxons had formed: “Bernicia and Deira in the north; Lindsey, Mercia, and Hwicce in the Midlands; East Anglia, Essex, and Kent along the eastern coast; and Sussex, Surrey, and Wessex in the south.”[7] Eventually these kingdoms merged into the Heptarchy for which this period is famous, consisting of the three greater kingdoms: Wessex (which expanded across the south, incorporating the Britons in the southwest in Cornwall and Devon as well), Northumbria (which was the merging of Bernicia and Deira), Mercia (which absorbed the smaller kingdoms south of the Humber river), and the minor kingdoms of East Anglia, Essex, Sussex, and Kent. All were ruled by different monarchs or dynasties (in the case of the minor kingdoms), and at one time or another were allies or enemies, looking to claim more power and territory from each other. [8]

The three major kingdoms all looked at one point that they would become the dominant power and unite the kingdoms under one rule; Northumbria in the seventh century and Mercia in the eighth century. But it was the House of Wessex that rose to the greatest power under King Egbert at the beginning of the ninth century. During his reign 802-839 CE Wessex expanded rapidly across the south. It benefitted from its strategic position and its growing wealth enabled the purchase of the best warriors and military technology. It also led the wars against the incoming Viking invasions, whose first raid on the island had taken place in 793 CE. [9]

Vikings, mainly from Denmark and Norway, raided and conquered territories in East Anglia, Essex and parts of Mercia and Northumbria between the 9th and 11th centuries. From 865 CE the Viking-settled region became known as Danelaw and was granted Danish self-rule in 884 CE under King Guthrum of Norway in exchange for his conversion to Christianity and leading his army out of Wessex. Eventually, the ongoing battles and attempts to expand territory on both sides resulted in the beginning of the breakup of Danelaw during Alfred’s reign. It was no until 902 CE when the region of Essex submitted to the rule of King Æthelwald that Danelaw fell apart rapidly. [10]

Alfred the Great, the grandson of King Egbert, became King of Wessex in 871. It was his drive to create a united England, and quell the growing power of the Danes, that led to him establishing a navy that would be able to fight the continuous invading Vikings at sea, build stronger defences on land, and fortresses across his territories. [11] Alfred’s descendants under the House of Wessex – his son Edward, daughter Ethelflaed (who governed Mercia as Lady of the Mercians when her husband Ethelred died), and his grandson, Æthelstan – continued to consolidate the power of Wessex by gaining territories across England. [12] The Kingdom of England was finally formed in 927 CE when all the independent kingdoms were finally unified as one country after under King Æthelstan of the House of Wessex.

Hierarchical Complexity[edit]

♠ Settlement hierarchy ♣ 6 ♥ levels.

Capital cities
Wessex - Winchester; Mercia - Tamworth; Northumbria - Bamburgh (north) and York (south – until 867 CE); Sussex - ; Essex - Colchester; East Anglia- tbc; Kent- tbc; Danelaw- York (from 867); Kingdom of England (from 927) - Winchester. These were the centres of royal and administrative power for each kingdom.
Major Towns
Important larger market towns such as London, Cambridge, and Ely. They had cathedrals and were the official seat of a diocesan bishop. [16][17]
Port and trading towns
Trading emporium and market towns and ports such as Dover, Sarre, Southampton, and Ipswich. [18][19]
Villages
Villages were mostly situated with access to good ploughing land. Many smaller, more isolated villages were in lower-status areas such as on the margin of occupied districts or in dry or chalky areas. These may have been used by the Germanic immigrants to establish themselves. Village or hamlet settlements were established close together, usually within 2-5 kilometres of each other.[20][21][22]
Hamlets
Hamlets were the smallest of settlements, which generally consisted of a few hall-type buildings. [23] Village or hamlet settlements were established close together, usually within 2-5 kilometres of each other.[24]
Monastic communities
Slightly more isolated and small communities based on a monastery with a self-sustaining ‘home-farm’ at its centre. [25] Double monasteries existed which housed monks and nuns side-by-side in communities ran by an abbess. [26]


♠ Administrative levels ♣ [1-4] ♥ levels.

Pre-sixth century

Warrior chief / early king
The earliest ‘kings’ in Anglo-Saxon England were essentially warrior chiefs who had their own personal followers, as in the tribes of northern Europe.[27] The actual nature of these warrior chiefs is dubious and there is not sufficient evidence to explain exactly how they governed or the hierarchy or administration that may have existed.[28]

Post-sixth century

King
It seems that kingship was not fully established in Anglo-Saxon ‘England’ until the mid-to-late sixth century. These are evidenced by increasingly rich male burials which are distinctive to the previously higher class of warrior burials. The arrival of Anglo-Saxon leaders resulted in the establishment of the first smaller kingdoms, which set aside any remaining power structures of the post-Roman Britons and started the beginnings of kingship which quickly became widespread throughout the region. [29][30] [31] Kings now governed through royal officials as outlined below.[32]
The Witan
A council made up of elected wise-men who acted as advisors, particularly for assisting the king on important state matters, new laws, land grants, or war strategy. The Whitan usually consisted of a collection of bishops, abbots, chaplains, ealdormen, royal family members and royal officials.[33]
Royal Officials
Port-Reeves
Port-Reeves were the royal officials in the Boroughs. [34]
Ealdormen
Ealdormen were the royal officials in the Shires. They were from a noble family and were the chief officer for the king in the shires. They preceeded over the bi-annual court. [35]
Baliffs
Baliffs became the royal official in the ‘Hundreds’ - smaller divisions of the Shires. They dealt with common criminal and civil matters at a monthly open court.[36]

♠ Military levels ♣ 3 ♥ levels.

King
Chief
Comitatus (later called Thegns)
Comitatus’ were pledged warriors who were employed by kings and chiefs.[42] “In a society where the success of a ruler and the people dependent upon him derived from effectiveness in war, the relationship of the king with his military followers was of vital importance. Tacitus saw the relationship of king and warband (comitatus) as central to the success and failure of the Germanic provinces he describes. The interaction between the king and his warriors is also a major concern of Old English heroic poetry. Poems like Beowulf stress the reciprocal nature of the relationship of king and comitatus. The followers fought loyally for their lord, but the loyalty had been purchased beforehand by the upkeep the king provided for his warriors and by the giving of gifts; conspicuous acts of loyalty in battle would be rewarded by further gifts-appropriate generosity was what made a ‘good king’. When not in battle, the king’s hall was the place where the necessary bonding of lord and follower occurred. The comitatus ate and slept in the hall at the king’s expense.” [43]
Fryd (standing army)
The fryd were a standing army always in service. They were divided up to protect the burghs and shires of the kingdom.[44]

Law[edit]

♠ Formal legal code ♣ absent_to_present ♥ The earliest surviving law codes from c. 600 CE for Kent state that the king, Æthelbert of Kent, had responsibility for law and order of all people within his kingdom. [48] Alfred the Great established a code of laws which incorporated laws from the other kingdoms in an attempt to create a central English law as part of his vision to unify the entire of England under one kingdom.[49]

♠ Judges ♣ inferred absent ♥ No reference to judge-type positions in the sources consulted. Rather, ealdormen, port-reeves or sheriffs presided over court matters.[50]

♠ Courts ♣ present ♥ Ealdormen in the shires presided over a bi-annual court which dealt with criminal, civil and ecclesiastical matters, and also declared new laws or dooms. From the eleventh century, ealdormen began to preside over several shires and so a subordinate, the shire-reeve (sherriff), took over court duties in the individual shires. [51]

♠ Professional Lawyers ♣ inferred absent ♥ No reference to lawyer-type positions in the sources consulted. Rather, ealdormen, port-reeves or sheriffs presided over court matters and plaintiff and defendants represented themselves.[52]
