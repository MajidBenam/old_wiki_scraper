General description[edit]

The British Empire consisted of the dominions, colonies, protectorates, mandates, and other territories ruled or administered by the United Kingdom. At its peak, at the end of the nineteenth century, it was the largest empire in history, comprising of territories of almost one-quarter of the worlds land surface, and a population that was one-quarter of the entire world’s population.

By 1858 the British Crown had taken full control of India from the East India Company after the mutiny and rebellion against the EIC in 1857. British India was then renamed as the British Raj. This lasted until the end of this polity period with the independence of India from the British Empire. A system of self-governance was gradually applied to some colonies after the independence of the American colonies. Dominion status was given to Canada (1867), Australia (1901), New Zealand (1907), the Union of South Africa (1910), and the Irish Free State (1921).

Following World Wars I and II, the call for independence for the British territories and colonies across the Empire gained momentum. The breakup of the Empire began in 1947 when India was granted full independence, quickly followed by Pakistan, Sri Lanka (then Ceylon) and Burma.

At the same time that the breakup of the empire began, The Commonwealth of Nations was established. The Commonwealth is a collective of countries - most of which were formally part of the British Empire - that are part of a voluntary political association, with Queen Elizabeth II as the head.

The remaining territories would not be granted independence until after the end of this polity period. African colonies gained independence starting with the Gold Coast in 1957. In 1997 the last major colony of Hong Kong was returned to China.

1965 - which happened to be the year that Winston Churchill died - is considered by some scholars to be the dividing line between colonial and contemporary Britain. Though remnants of the Empire still remained at this point (and in fact small overseas territories are still held by Britain to this day) most major holdings had already been ceeded back to the authority of the local country, and the British Empire was considered to be dissolved.
