General Description[edit]

The Ptolemaic Kingdom (or Empire) was one of the successor states to the Macedonian Empire created by the conquests of Alexander the Great in the late 4th century BCE. When Alexander died in Babylon in 323, Ptolemy, as one of his most favoured generals and bodyguards, was appointed satrap (governor) of Egypt, Libya and parts of Arabia.[1][2] The next few decades after 323 were characterized by incessant warfare between those who wished to maintain the unity of the Macedonian Empire, nominally still intact,[3] and those who aspired to rule their own kingdoms independently.[4] Ptolemy was firmly on the separatist side, and in 305 BCE he successfully declared himself king of Egypt. In doing so, he became Ptolemy I Soter ('the saviour')[5], founder of a powerful dynasty (sometimes known as the Lagides, after his father Lagos)[6] that was to rule Egypt for almost three centuries.
Ptolemy I and his successors had expansionist ambitions, seeking to carve out more and more territory for their new kingdom, often at the expense of the other kingdoms that had splintered from Alexander's empire, especially the Seleucid Kingdom of the Middle East.[7] At its greatest extent, the Ptolemaic Empire reached as far south as Lower Nubia (southern Egypt), west to Cyrenaica (modern-day Libya), east to Cyprus, Syria, Phoenicia and Asia Minor (Turkey), and north into the Aegean.[8] In the words of one researcher, Egypt became for the first time a true 'Mediterranean power' under its new Macedonian rulers.[9]
The peak of the Ptolemaic period is generally considered to correspond to the reigns of the first three Ptolemies in the 3rd century BCE.[10] We divide the kingdom into two polities: the first begins with Ptolemy I's accession in 305 and ends with the Battle of Raphia in 217. In this battle, Ptolemy IV defeated the Seleucid king Antiochus III, who had invaded Ptolemaic-controlled lands in Palestine.[11] The late 3rd and early 2nd centuries saw conflict within the ruling family and revolts by the Egyptian population, representing an 'age of crisis' between two periods of relative stability.[12] Our second polity runs from 217 up to the famous suicide of Cleopatra VII, the last ruler in the Ptolemaic line, and the Roman annexation of Egypt in 30 BCE.[13][14] Overall, the Ptolemies were a successful dynasty: in concert with their expansionist policies, they managed to transform Egypt ‒ and the new city of Alexandria in particular ‒ into the cultural and economic centre of the Hellenistic world.[15]

Population and Political Organization

The Ptolemies were the longest-lived foreign dynasty ever to rule Egypt.[16] They presided over a 'double society', portraying themselves as Graeco-Macedonian kings to the many resident Greeks and divine pharaohs to the 'native' Egyptian population.[17][18] Greeks and Egyptians were subject to different judicial systems and Greeks tended to dominate the highest echelons of society.[19][20] Alexandria, built as an ideal Greek-style Hellenistic city with its magnificent library, stadium, theatre, gymnasium and lighthouse, was always set apart from the rest of the country.[21][22] Over time, however, and especially from 200 BCE onwards, the boundaries between 'Greek' and 'Egyptian' became blurred.[23]
The chief aim of government was to draw as much revenue ‒ in money and in wheat ‒ as possible from the population, and for this reason the burden of taxation was heavy.[24] The Ptolemies left many Pharaonic Egyptian institutions intact, such as the temple hierarchy with its priests and scribes. However, they used state functionaries and tax farmers to divert more and more wealth from temples, agricultural estates, especially those of granted to soldiers (known as cleruchs), and ordinary peasant farmers to the royal coffers.[25] Egypt under the Ptolemies also became more outward-looking, extending commercial and political power into the Levant, the Black Sea and the shores of the Mediterranean as far west as Sicily.[26]
The population of Egypt during the Ptolemaic period has been estimated at around 4 million people in the 3rd century BCE, of which between 5 and 10 percent were Greeks.[27] The total population of the entire Ptolemaic Empire may have reached 7 million.[28]

♠ Religious levels ♣ 1 ♥

There were many different sizes/classes of temples and a multi-level prestige hierarchy. However, a prestige hierarchy does not represent hierarchical levels of authority within a religious organization. The code therefore might look like this "5 King, 4 High Priest of Ptah of Memphis, 3 regional chief priest, 2 Lector Priest, 1 Wab priest" but in fact these were only titles; the lower ranked priests were independent and did not take orders from those higher in rank. Since this code does not code prestige hierarchy only hierarchical levels of authority we code 1.

Certain priesthoods, e.g. the High Priest of Ptah at Memphis, exerted considerable political influence. Functioned as a virtual centralized priesthood in Egypt; several of the synodal meetings of priests from throughout Egypt met at Memphis, from which emanated the trilingual decrees, e.g. the Rosetta Stone. These priests were closely associated with the priests at Letopolis. See D. Thompson, Memphis under the Ptolemies. 2d. ed. Princeton, 2012.

The dynastic cult was founded by Ptolemy II. The annually selected priesthoods of the cult were based in Alexandria, and later also in the southern capital at Ptolemais. Within the dynastic cult there is not much of a hierarchy as there is not a command structure. The Egyptian cults are completely separated. Here each temple functions autonomously. There is a clear hierarchy within each temple.

Professions[edit]

♠ Occupational complexity ♣ ♥

♠ Professional military officers ♣ present ♥

Salaried officers, who were members of both the Greek and Egyptian elites, and mercenaries were prevalent in Ptolemaic Egypt. Their salaries reflected the rank they held. The higher officers (who were friends of the king) were professionals in the sense that they held no other functions. These higher officers were dispersed over the settlement towns and garrison towns. [68]

♠ Professional soldiers ♣ present ♥

"The standing army ... was made up of professional soldiers stationed in garrisons and cleruchic troops serving, in turn, presumably for only part of the year, in garrisons. All cleruchic troops were mobilized simultaneously only in case of war. In addition, the existence of a mixed group, the misthophoroi klerouchoi, shows that the system was flexible."[69]

"professional or mercenary soldiers received cash (opsonion or misthos) and food, whereas cleruchs were granted plots of land (kleroi)."[70]

♠ Professional priesthood ♣ present ♥

Full-time priests who held not other functions were common in Ptolemaic Egypt. They were the ones who conducted the rituals. However, there were only a few of those priests for every temple.

♠ Professional Lawyers ♣ present ♥ present: 168-30 BCE

Professional advocates are certainly documented in the second century bc. Among the more noteworthy aspects of the technical details revealed in a famous Demotic archive are the proceedings of a trial held at Asyut in the middle of the second century bc is the complexity of the Ptolemaic legal system. That complexity was caused by two main factors. First, the bureaucratic system operated in two languages, Greek and Demotic. Greek, or at least officials with Greek names, dominated state administrative offices (e.g. the epistates, the stratêgoi) while Egyptians, and the Egyptian language, dominated local temple administration. It is all of this complexity that I think provides us with one of the most interesting historical facts coming from this archive. The judges asked Chratianch the plaintiff: "Is there a man who speaks for you?" A man appears, with a non-Egyptian name, to answer a few technical points on behalf of the plaintiff. He was not a guardian (or a kurios in the Greek sense) because he would have been identified as such. Rather he is simply called a man, but it is difficult not to conclude that he was in fact functioning as an advocate on behalf of the woman. On an unrelated petition on the verso of the text that records the trial and its outcome, Tuot son of Petihor was specifically mentioned as an advocate for the priests of Isis at Aswan. This same man may have been involved in the recorded trial, and indeed this archive may have been his. Much is uncertain. But if it is a Ptolemaic institution, a second question emerges. Was it merely the result of the complexities of the Ptolemaic system, or did it develop under Greek influence? Finally, it seems clear that such reports of trials and the use of advocates, better documented from the Roman period, have their origin in the Ptolemaic bureaucratic administration of trials (Joe will write a paragraph with more details).

The largest scale collective ritual of the dynastic cult was the Ptolemaieia. A major festival introduced by Ptolemy II (285-246 BC) to honour his parents, Ptolemy I and Berenike I[158]. The Ptolemaic period witnessed the introduction of the dynastic cult as an official source of royal legitimacy through stressing on and tracing back familial links between the living ruling king and his divine male and female royal predecessors[159]. The foundations of the cult were laid by Ptolemy I (305-282 BC) when he elevated Alexander the Great to the level of gods and announced his priest, the so-called eponymous priest, as the highest priest in the land. By associating himself with the cult of Alexander, Ptolemy procured divinity for himself and for the Ptolemaic dynasty. Later, Ptolemy II (282-246 BC) placed his parents as "saviour gods", whereas the king and his sister-wife Arsinoe II (317-268 BC) were declared the "brother-sister loving" gods.

The festival followed the model of the major festivals of the Greek world celebrated at Olympia in honour of Zeus. It constituted of a) procession, b) sacrifice: two thousand bulls were sacrificed, c) isolympic competitions in athletics, music, and equestrian d) two banquets, one reserved for the high ranking international guests and the other for soldiers, members of cultic associations, and visitors [160]. It involved 2,240 men pulling carts, 12,000 were involved in the procession and 80,800 army troops (57,600 infantry, 23,200 cavalry)[161]. The audience was the whole polity, the highly ranking distinguished invited guests from all over the Greek world and the occasional tourists.[EWA: every 5th year, and rephrase for the whole polity, replace by Alexandria focused]. It was celebrated in Alexandria every four years. The festival was perhaps first celebrated in the winter of 279-278 BC, marking the penteteric anniversary of the death of Ptolemy I[162].

Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ daily offering ♥ "The daily offering ritual was performed in every functioning temple three times a day, in imitation of human meal times. [...] The offering ritual is known from the Old Kingdom, from the Pyramid Texts and the economic records of Neferirkare at Abu Sir, and it continued to be the essential temple ritual throughout Egyptian history. "The ritual consisted of the physical maintenance of the god, followed by the presentation of food and drink. All ritual actions were made in the name of the king, and it is he who is shown in the temple reliefs, although in reality a priest acted in his stead. Each morning as the sun rose, the temple staff would assemble for duty. The daily ritual involved a variety of ranks of priests - wabs, lector priests, and God's Fathers are specifically mentioned. The Chief Priest would be specially purified in a section of the temple called the per duat or 'the house of morning.' There, he washed his body with water, was purifed with incense, and rinsed his mouth with natron. Texts record, 'There was performed for him all ceremonies that are performed for the king,' transforming him from a priest into a proper surrogate for the king. He then joined the other priests, who had also undergone purification. They gathered the materials needed for the ritual and proceeded to the temple's sanctuary. Before entering, they began a series of recitations that accompanied their preparations - the utterance for lighting the fire; the utterances for taking the incense, putting the brazier on the censer, and putting incense on the fire; and, finally, the 'utterance for advancing to the holy place.' The sense that the ritual was in theory performed by the king is evidence in the Chief Priest's first ritual words: 'It is the king who sends me.' "Each step of the ritual was highly symbolic, referred to mythic events, and was accompanied by a set liturgy. To begin the ritual, the priest awakened the god who slept in the sealed shrine. Holding a candle, he entered the sanctuary, chanting, 'Awake in peace! May your awakening be peaceful!' He broke the seals of the door bolts of the shrine, and drew back the bolts - an action that was equated with removing the fingers of Seth from the eye of Horus. The priest then 'opened the sight of the god' as he swung open the doors of the shrine, and he 'kissed the ground,' prostrating himself before the god's shrine. Each ritual step was punctuated with a specific recitation and an offering of incense and adoration of the deity. In preparation for removing the god from his shrine, the priest scattered pure white sand on the floor of the sanctuary, symbolizing the mound of creation from which all life sprang. He then recited a liturgy for 'Laying Hands Upon the God,' and removed the statue from the shrine, placing it on the sand. After more purifications, the priest removed the deity's outer linen garments and jewelry and wiped away the unguents from the previous day's ritual. Once the statue was suitably cleansed, the priest offered the god lengths of red, white, and green cloth that symbolized the blood of Isis, the uraeus, and fertility. This was followed by the presentation of green and black eye cosmetics. The god was adorned with bracelets, a broad collar, and anklets, and given the appropriate scepters and headdress. The statue was then wrapped in his 'great garment', probably a linen wrapper such as those that were found with the divine statues in the tomb of Tutankhamun. "Now fully awakened and ready for the day, the god was presented with flowers, food, and drink. Economic texts specify that the food offerings consisted of grain, vegetables, wine, and cuts of meat and fowl. When the god was considered to be done, an interval that is not specified in the texts, the food was taken away and the god was readied for sleep. After further purifications, the god was returned to his shrine, the doors closed and seal. As a final ritual, the king performed the 'Bringing of the Foot,' in which he grasped a broomlike had-plant and backed out of the sanctuary, sweeping his footprints from the room. This procedure not only left the sanctuary in good order, but, it was believed, also prevented evil from approaching the god, presumably by following the footprints of the priest-king. "A version of this ritual was repeated at midday and in the evening. The evidence for how elaborate these other two daily rituals were is inconclusive, but it is clear that the morning service was the most complete. The evening service may have been essentially food offerings, but the shrine was not opened, and the activity took place in the antechamber rather than directly before the god." [215]

Most euphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ Symposia ♥ The most euphoric collective ritual of Ptolemaic Egypt was the drinking-symposia. The symposium was a collective after dinner ritual for men which focused mainly on communal drinking[255]. Symposia occurred at both royal and private levels [256]. Groups of nobles, highly ranking army officers, court members and wealthy individuals would gather in the evening either in the royal palace or an elite house to mainly drink wine, yet the gathering served as a social forum for elite men to debate, plot, boast, and revel with others. They were frequently held to celebrate the introduction of young men into society. Symposia were also held to celebrate other special occasions, such as victories in athletic and poetic contests. Through this ritual elites affirmed their status and class unity[257]. Drinking-symposia was practised by the Greek elites in Ptolemaic Egypt. For example, a great mass of East Greek imported pottery and decorated kylix cups retrieved from Naukratis, the Greek settlement established by the Ptolemies, which suggests that drinking symposia took place there[258] Also early third century papyrus from Elephantine in Upper Egypt, records symposia as a living practice [259] The ritual involved mainly excessive drinking of wine mixed with water. The symposium was inaugurated by a libation and unmixed wine tasting, then three bowls of wine were mixed; delicacies were served, hymns were sung, a myrtle branch being passed from singer to singer. Other features of the symposium included sympotic table talks, reclining on couches, homosexual courtship, drinking contests such as flicking wine from cup. Hosts provided attractive flute girls and cup-bearing boys to amuse the guests and respectable women were excluded[260]. It is important to recognise that the drinking of wine was associated with Dionysus and immoderate drinking of wine was considered as a tribute to him. Thus, the effect of wine of the symposium participants was important. The wine expressed the qualities of Dionysus and his power to inspire and raise the spirits. Through the growing intensity of the wine, the advancing stages of drunkenness revealed the attributes of the god himself. The stages of intensity range from well being to love to desire to wantonness to deeds of violence. However, the amount and strength of wine drunk was regulated by the host [261]. Following the symposium, participants would go out in the streets as revellers, acting out the madness that accompanies the drinking of Dionysus wine. They were accompanied by slaves with torches and perhaps bars. They would parade through the streets insulting the people that they passed in a state of ritual inversion[262]. NOTE: another candidate for most euphoric is the procession of Hathor. The most euphoric collective ritual was the annual marriage procession of Hathor of Dendera to Edfu. These two temples are approximately 80 miles apart from each other. This procession attracted large crowds who came from all over Upper Egypt. Note: Manning comments: "Most widespread??"[263] The activities involved were seeing the god (who is normally hidden), drinking, eating, singing, dancing. We know that there was special food to be had. There were no special activities of a sexual nature.

Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ mystery cults ♥ Most dysphoric ritual during the Ptolemaic period are perhaps the mystic rituals linked with what is known today as mystic cults. These mystic rituals are associated with a number of deities such as Demeter, Dionysus, the Great Gods, the Mother, the Mistress, Isis and Mithras[267]. The rites were normally carried by night, and in secret. They involved dysphoric features of frightening and disorienting, rapid movement between dark and light, loud music and other noise[268]. The cults were open to everyone to take part in once they were initiated. Initiations were performed as a rite of passage by specialists who acquired copies of ritual texts and other sacred objects. Initiators were often treated with respect among society [269] These cults were characteristic of the Hellenistic world, yet, little is known about how these rites were developed and practised during the Ptolemaic period. Evidence show that mysteries were introduced to Ptolemaic Egypt from as early as Ptolemy I(305-282 BC). The king is said to have invited Timotheus, a member of the Eumolpidae, one of the priestly families of the mystic cult of Eleusis, to come to Alexandria in order to introduce mystic rituals there [270] This theory could be supported by the existence of a suburb called Eleusis in Ptolemaic Alexandria which suggests mystic rites were perhaps performed in the cosmopolitan city. The two main mystic cults which could be more closely linked to Ptolemaic Egypt are those of Dionysus and Isis. During the reign of Ptolemy IV (221-205 BC) a decree was issued controlling the activities of individuals who performed mystic rites for Dionysus[271]. The document reveals the existence of private religious practitioners related to the mystic rites of Dionysus. In this decree the individuals involved in Dionysus' mystic cult are asked to register themselves in Alexandria and declare from whom they have learnt the rites or received the sacred objects as far as three generations back, and hand in a sealed copy of their sacred text. This decree reveals the state's attempt to monitor the rites and guarantee its authenticity by tracing its origin either through the texts or the individuals who have passed it on to others[272]. Yet, apart from this decree no other evidence of the msytic cult of Dionysus in Ptolemaic Egypt has been found. On the other hand, plentiful of evidence for the cult of Isis have been retrieved from all over the Mediterranean world, yet little clear evidence for the nature of the cult could be established [273]. It is possible that mystery cult of Isis started during the third century when the goddess was introduced to Asia Minor as well as other Greek islands associated with Serapis. Serapis was a newly developed god by Ptolemy I which provided linkage between Osiris and Apis. The spread of the cult of Isis around the Greek world was closely linked to the political and military activities of the Ptolemaic kings, it would generally have been city councils rather than individuals who were responsible for the introduction of the Isis cult. Thus, due to the political motives behind the spread of the Isis cult outside Egypt not all her cults could qualify as being mystic [274]. ♠ Ideological reinforcement of monogamy ♣ present ♥ “Greek culture as a whole was monogamous in the sense that a man was expected to have only one wife at a time (the wife being the bearer of his legitimate heirs). In general, the philosophers said that sexual desire was something men must suppress in order to achieve the good life; they thought that friendship was the most important value and it should always take precedence over sex. [...] The bottom line is that monogamy was the cultural expectation (supported by religion in the sense that even Zeus and Poseidon and Hades only had one wife each). Normative Ideological Aspects of Slavery[edit]

♠ Ideology forbids slavery for co-ethnics ♣ absent ♥ “I am not aware of any explicit condemnation of slavery as an institution by any ancient philosopher, nor any movement, large or small, to abolish slavery. Both Stoics and Epicureans made much of the idea that ‘true slavery’ is not external but an internal matter: whether one is a slave to one’s desires and emotions. Thus every slave can be ‘free’ and every master can be “enslaved” depending on their character. Even Epictetus, who was a slave himself, did not advocate the abolishment of slavery (nor of course did the Christians). It seems to have been unthinkable to them, given how embedded it was in the social and economic structure of their society. Traditional Greco-Roman religion had nothing doctrinal to say about slavery as a social institution, so far as I can tell; in practice it was accepted and not regarded as problematic. It is true that sanctuaries sometimes owned slaves, just as they owned land or other forms of property. During the Hellenistic period, religious devotion to certain deities was conceptualized as a form of willing slavery; this is known in Ptolemaic Egypt, for example. People would ‘enslave’ themselves for a period to gods like Sarapis.” [374]

♠ care/harm ♣ present ♥ “The philosophers taught various versions of the Golden Rule, whereas traditional Greek morality said it was best to help one’s friends and harm one’s enemies. The only philosopher I can think of who specifically advocates “helping people” over “living luxuriously” is the Stoic Musonius Rufus from the first century CE, but he may have been an innovator in that respect. In general the Greeks had no religious or philosophical teachings to compare with Jewish and Christian teachings about almsgiving, gleaning, or caring for “widows and orphans.” Greek cities sometimes gave stipends to orphans if their fathers had died in battle defending the city. The most important traditional religious teaching on this subject was that the gods required people to treat “strangers and suppliants” well. That is, you should assist strangers who come to your door in need (and definitely not harm them). You can see this when Odysseus disguised as a beggar receives hospitality (Homer was a basic school text in the Hellenistic period) or in the Hellenistic myth of Baucis and Philemon, a very poor elderly couple who received two strangers and gave them hospitality. The strangers turned out to be Zeus and Hermes, who rewarded the couple. The belief that the gods “tested” humans by coming down to earth was common Hellenistic Asia Minor, where Paul and Barnabas were mistaken for Zeus and Hermes in disguise (Acts 14). Hellenistic philanthropy was closely tied to piety because the benefactions were usually things like sponsoring a festival or enhancing a sanctuary. Sponsoring a festival meant entertainment and free food distribution, but the main goal was not necessarily to help the poor—it was more to enhance the public good. I think it is very likely that the teachings of philosophers encouraged these sorts of activities, but of course the donors also benefited from increased prestige.” [426]

We have already seen that Euripides, one of the most sensitive and innovative intellectuals of the Classical period, criticized both the undifferentiated application of the ritual of supplication and the lack of a distinction between a pure body and pure thoughts. This suggests that the development of the idea of the purity of the mind and its priority over the purity of the body was part of a more general process of fundamental changes in the perception of rituals in Classical and Hellenistic Greece. This process leads from the traditional view that ritual actions are effective when correctly performed to the idea that a ritual’s efficacy depends (also) on the moral and other justifications of the individual who performs it. We may observe this process from the late fifth to the third centuries BCE in measures for the exclusion of criminals from asylum, in the development of a new type of curses, in moral requirements for initiation into mystery cults, and more generally in a tendency towards a priority of the word and the thought over the ritual action.” [429]
♠ kinship ♣ present ♥ “Piety required respect for one’s parents, especially in regard to making the annual offerings once they were dead. To mistreat a parent or neglect a parental tomb was a sign of moral depravity. Greek city-states legislated that children owed their parents food, lodging and burial (this goes back to Solon, the first lawgiver at Athens); those who failed in this duty could have their citizenship rights revoked. The worst crimes imaginable were incest with a parent and killing one’s own parent. Killing one’s own child did not carry the same weight of horror, although any kin-murder was considered more serious than murder of non-kin. The expectations for ritual purification after killing made these types of distinctions, and they could be regarded as a form of “regulation.” The Greeks recognized both written and unwritten laws. In general, the need to obey and respect parents was much, much stronger than any parental obligation to a child. Greek fathers throughout antiquity had the right to “expose” offspring they rejected at birth, which meant abandoning the infant either in the wild or in a place known to be used for that purpose, where it might or might not be picked up by slave traders. This did not count as murder, although once the infant was acknowledged by the father and accepted into the household, that would be a different matter. The Kyrene religious law code of the 4th century BCE says that “a man is bound as far as his brother’s children,” meaning that religious obligation (such as paying a fine levied for religious offense on a nephew) extended that far, and only on the paternal side.” [430]

Disease

♠ Endemic disease ♣ present ♥ Several studies of skeletal remains indicate the presence of, e.g., dental caries,[463] cancer[464], malaria,[465], and endemic syphilis,[466] while tests performed on mummified remains indicate the presence of leprosy[467] and tuberculosis.[468] Venereal syphilis is not proven; if present, relatively low prevalence.[469] Relatively uncommon, but present in elite populations is atherosclerosis.[470]
♠ Disease outbreaks ♣ present ♥ Historical records make it clear that there were disease outbreaks, and Greek immigrants are thought to have brought certain disease strains with them. "Epidemic diseases, named in Egyptian texts as the demonic 'arrows of Sakhmet' and other gods, were associated with specific times of year, notably just before the New Year and the inundation."[471] Identifying these disease strains is quite difficult, and while measles has been mostly ruled out as being too young from an evolutionary standpoint,[472] smallpox is thought to have been present, and, due to its high mortality rate, the likely cause of major disease epidemics.[473]
