England had been a Catholic country since the Anglo-Saxon period. However, in 1534, Henry VIII officially broke with the Roman Catholic church and established the Church of England and made himself the Supreme Head of the church. [1] While his children and successors, Edward VI (protestant), Mary I (Catholic) and Elizabeth I (protestant) changed the official religion of England on their ascension to the throne, England remained a protestant country thereafter.

General description[edit]

Early modern England is generally accepted to span the late fifteenth to eighteenth centuries, from the beginning of Henry VII’s reign and the start of the Tudor period in 1485 until around 1717 with the Treaty of Union (1707) in which Scottish and English (including Welsh) parliaments and sovereign were combined, and the end of the reign of the last Stuart monarch, Queen Anne, in 1714.

This period incorporates immense changes in technology, science, medicine, demographics as well as seeing the creation of the British Empire and its widespread colonisation of countries around the world.

Social Scale[edit]

♠ Polity territory ♣ 1485: 130,279 ; 1714: 209,331 ♥ in squared kilometers. The territory of the polity (England and Wales) at the beginning of the period was 130,279 km2. By the end of the polity period in 1714, the Union Act had been created which pulled Scotland into the government and territory of the crown as well, making it a total of 209,331 km2.[3]

♠ Polity Population ♣ 1485: 2,200,000 ; 1714: 9,500,000 ♥ People. England (including the English-ruled Wales) had a population of around 2.2million in 1485. [4] By 1714 the combined British Isle territories of England, Wales, and Scotland (as Great Britain) had a population of 9.5 million. [5]


♠ Population of the largest settlement ♣ 1485: 50,000 ; 1700: 500,000 ♥ Inhabitants. London was by far the most densely populated city in England – indeed it was the largest in Europe. The population of the city was 50,000 in 1485 and grew to around 500,000 inhabitants by 1700. The next largest cities of Norwich, Bristol, Coventry and York had less than 10,000 inhabitants each.[6]

♠ Largest communication distance ♣ 901 ♥ in kilometers. From London to the northernmost city of Inverness in Scotland is 901 kilometres. However, this is by modern roads so the route taken during this polity period is likely to have been much longer. Awaiting to see if further sources reveal the true distance. [7]

♠ Administrative levels ♣ [1-6] ♥ levels. The governmental structure of the early modern period continued over from the preceding period of Plantagenet England.

1. King
The king was at the very top of all social and administrative hierarchies. However by this period he was more accountable to his council and the aristocracy, though ultimately, his decision on all state matters was final.[13] The kings council usually consisted of a mixture of trusted nobles, experienced officials (usually the chancellor and treasurer), ecclesiastical members, judges and sometimes a close and trusted member of his family.[14]

Administrative duties were divided between the King’s Household, and the two central administrative departments: the exchequer and the chancery. The law courts were also an integral part of government. State and local departments were not truly separated, but they were hierarchical in the local officials reported into direct lines to the main state department.[15]

2. The King’s Household

The Kings Household was an administrative department that was responsible for a variety of duties and was a central hub of the government. It provided for the domestic and organisational needs of the court as well as [16]

2.1 The Wardrobe
The wardrobe was the financial department of the king’s household and at times would out-rank the exchequer in terms of financial hierarchy. Orders issued under the privy seal to the exchequer and chancery came from the wardrobe. By the reign of Edward III the exchequer was recognised as the head department of financial administration, though when it suited the king, the wardrobe would be used to issue huge sums of money sum – without the consent of the exchequer – such as during the French War in the 1330’s and military campaigns. [17]
2.2 Clerks of the Household
Responsible for the organisation of war, recruitment of soldiers and upper management, pay and board.[18]


2. The Exchequer

“The exchequer was divided into two sections, the lower, which dealt with money paid in and the issue if receipts in the form of wooden tallies, and the upper, which was the court where accounts were rendered and heard.”[19] It assigned revenue. [20]

2. Chancery

The chancery issued writs and in the early period was closely linked to the King’s Household. However after being divided between Gascony and England during Edward I’s campaign in the 1280’s, the chancery became increasingly separate from the royal household and no longer followed the king around as the Household did and by Edward III’s reign was based permanently at Westminster in London.[29]

2.1 Chancellor[30]
2.2 Sergeants[31]
2.3 Masters
Twelve ‘Masters’ (senior clerks) ran the department and managed the clerks, curistors, and assistant clerks and servants below them.[32]
2.4 Clerks
By the fourteenth century around one hundred clerks were employed. Records from 1324 show that they were producing about eighty writs and day – 29,000 in that year. [33]
2.5 Curistors
Those who wrote standardised writs.[34]
2.6 Assistant Clerks[35]

The law courts were divided between the King’s Bench and the Common Pleas.

2. The King’s Bench
The King’s Bench travelled with the king in order to action any necessary legal work immediately. It also dealt with appeals from the lower courts and criminal matters.[36]
2. The Common Pleas
The Common Pleas was the court based permanently at Westminster and dealt mainly with property matters. [37]

Law[edit]

♠ Formal legal code ♣ present ♥ There were polity-wide laws that were maintained and issued by the Chancery department. [54]

♠ Judges ♣ present ♥ [55]

♠ Courts ♣ present ♥ There were law courts and ecclesiastical courts. The church would deal with local misdemeanours such as drunkenness, adultery and blaspheming. The Chancery and Exchequer had their own courts. In London there were the common law courts of the King’s Bench (for civil and criminal cases pertaining to the Crown) and Common Pleas (for civil suits such as debt, property or contract disputes.)[56]

♠ Professional Lawyers ♣ present ♥ Lawyers were professionally trained and could make up to £4,000 per year if they worked directly for the crown. [57]

Kinds of Written Documents

♠ Lists, tables, and classifications ♣ present ♥ Division lists were kept by parliament as to how people voted. [78]
♠ Calendar ♣ present ♥ England used the Julian calendar throughout this polity period. They did not adopt the Gregorian calendar until the mid-eighteenth century. [79]
♠ Sacred Texts ♣ present ♥ The Bible.
♠ Religious literature ♣ present ♥ Religious guidance and sermons were often published throughout the period. Most notably Sacheverell’s The Perils of False Brethren (1709). Issac Newton also wrote commentaries on the bible in the seventeenth century. [80] The Society for the Propagation of Christian Knowledge, founded in 1698, circulated religious literature to the colonies and for at-home learning.[81]
♠ Practical literature ♣ present ♥ Advice books for subjects such as agricultural and farming. Travel books. [82]
♠ History ♣ present ♥ Written history became increasingly popular among the upper-classes and educated during this period. Thomas More’s A History of Richard III (in English in 1543). Sir Walter Ralegh wrote a history of the world (1614).
♠ Philosophy ♣ present ♥ Philosophy was increasingly popular among the upper class and educated during this period.[83]Elizabth I translated On the Consolations of Philosophy into English. [84]
♠ Scientific literature ♣ present ♥ Science was increasingly popular from the Stuart period, most notably with Charles II being a patron of the sciences. Laboratories and The Royal Society published many scientific papers and books. Sir Francis Bacon wrote Advancement of Learning (1605) which laid the groundwork for scientific method among many other scientific volumes. In 1687 Newton alongside the Royal Society published Principa Mathematica: or the Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy.[85]
♠ Fiction ♣ present ♥ Poetry and the arts became increasingly popular among the upper-classes and educated during this period. The English sonnet was developed by Sir Thomas Wyatt in the early sixteenth century. Thomas More’s Utopia (1516) and Francis Bacon’s New Atlantis (1626) were utopian novels of socio-political satire. Some monarchs, including Henry VIII and Elizabeth I, wrote poetry. Famous playwrights such as Shakespeare, as well as successful women like Aphra Behn (1640-89) and Susannah Centlivre (1669-1723).[86]

Other[edit]

Money

♠ Articles ♣ inferred absent ♥ There is no mention in the sources that articles were used as a form of currency, and by this period the English currency system was well established.
♠ Tokens ♣ absent_to_present ♥ From the seventeenth century tokens were sometimes issued by pubs and inns as a form of currency.
♠ Precious metals ♣ present ♥ Gold.
♠ Foreign coins ♣ inferred absent ♥ There is no mention in the sources that foreign coins were circulating at this time, and by this period the English currency system was well established.
♠ Indigenous coins ♣ present ♥ Coins were minted by each monarch throughout the period. English currency included guineas, pounds, shillings and pennies. [87]
♠ Paper currency ♣ absent ♥ Bonds and bills of exchange were issued on paper but it was not currency.[88]
♠ Debt and credit structures ♣ present ♥ The monarch could raise money through parliament and his supporters though they were almost constantly in debt in some form or another. By 1700 a credit facility for bonds and bills of exchange had been established. [89] Banks were established fully by the end of this polity period (By the 1720’s there were twenty-five in London.) [90]
♠ Store of wealth ♣ present ♥ Wealthy individuals, including the monarchy, often kept stores of precious metal, money, plate, precious jewels etc in their homes. Banks were well established in the seventeenth century. [91]

Postal System

♠ Couriers ♣ present ♥ Monarchs used couriers to govern their vast kingdoms from a distance. [92]
♠ Postal stations ♣ absent_to_present ♥ Postal stations could be found at inns from the late seventeenth century. [93]
♠ General postal service ♣ absent_to_present ♥ The English postal service was established during Charles II’s reign (1660-1685). The penny post was established by 1700. [94]
♠ Fastest individual communication ♣ suspected unknown ♥ There is no direct mention of the fastest communication in the sources consulted, however it would have been by ship, along the internal waterways or along the coast, or via horse relay.
