General Description[edit]

The Qing Dynasty (or Empire of the Great Qing, Great Qing, Manchu Dynasty, Manchus, Jin, Jurchens, Ch'ing Dynasty) was China's last imperial dynasty. The founders of the Qing were descendants of Jurchen Jin rulers. The dynasty was founded by Nurhaci and then led by his son Huang Taiji, but did not become an imperial Chinese dynasty until after Huang Taiji's death.[2] In 1644 CE, Qing forces captured the Ming capital at Beijing from rebels and held a funeral for the last Ming emperor to symbolize Qing inheritance of the Mandate of Heaven.[3]
The Qing faced conflict with rebels and loyalist Ming forces for the next two decades.[4] Ming generals who surrendered were given power over large territories in southern China in exchange for loyalty to the Qing. In 1673 CE, leaders from three major southern feudatories led by Wu Sangui rebelled against Emperor Kangxi when he tried to reduce their power.[5] The Revolt of the Three Feudatories, as this episode is known, lasted eight years.
We divide the Qing Dynasty into two, an Early period (1644-1796 CE) and a Late period (1796-1912 CE). The division is marked by a period of internal turmoil as well as foreign incursions into its territory and economic sphere. In the Early Qing period, China had been prosperous under Kangxi and Qing rule, but by the time of the Opium Wars in the Late Qing, Western technology and industry had surpassed that of China.[6] The fall of the Qing Dynasty in 1911 gave rise to the Republic of China.
From 1850 to 1864 CE, China was racked by the fourteen-year Taiping Rebellion. The rebellion directly caused 30 million deaths and destroyed many regions in the middle and lower Yangtze.[7] In 1853, the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace established a capital in Nanjing, but the rebellion was defeated by armies led by local governors in 1864.[8] A number of serious uprisings followed the Taiping Rebellion, including the Nian Rebellion (1853-1868 CE).[9]
At the same time, the Qing emperors were facing economic problems due to the actions of foreign powers. In the 1830s, British merchants began illegally importing opium to China, where high demand for the drug led to a large trade imbalance. China's economy was drained of silver[10] and the value of copper coins depreciated.[11] The First Opium War broke out in 1839 CE when a Chinese commissioner attempted to block opium trade in Guangzhou harbour.[12] The Second Opium War of 1858 CE was a series of military actions by the British and French against the Qing.[13] The resulting treaties allowed foreign powers to establish concessions in China, abolished taxes for French and British merchants, and forced the Qing to pay large amounts of silver in damages.[14]
The 19th century saw increasingly frequent intrusions by foreign powers. Foreign merchants exploited their tax-free status, to the detriment of local Chinese producers. China was forced to cede much of its territory in Vietnam, Burma and elsewhere.[15] By the end of the 19th century, a range of foreign powers including Great Britain, Japan, Germany, and France claimed colonial territories in China.[16] A peasant uprising known as the Boxer Rebellion targeted foreigners in 1900 CE.
In 1860, the Qing rulers were exiled outside the Great Wall when foreign invaders burned down the Summer Palace.[17] The court was restored by the regent Empress Dowager Cixi and Prince Gong in what is known as the Tongzhi restoration.[18] However, the dynasty was finally overthrown in the Revolution of 1911 and the Republic of China was founded.

Population and political organization

The Late Qing maintained a traditional imperial-style Chinese government headed by an emperor and central bureaucracy. Provincial government consisted of governors who controlled a hierarchical system of officials, prefects, county chiefs, county magistrates, and clerks.[19] The Qing were deeply opposed to modernization: the scholars Kang Youwei and Liang Qichao had to flee after attempting to reform government practices in 1898 CE.[20] Rebellions in the 19th century led to the rise of local governors and military commanders, who acted as warlords to control their local regions.[21]
The period between the mid-18th and mid-19th centuries was one of extremely rapid population growth in Late Qing China, and by 1851 the population had reached 431.9 million people.[22] However, a number of censuses after that date could not be completed due to the rebellions.

Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ palace exams ♥ Civil service exams were taken at four progressive levels: local exams; provincial exams; metropolitan exams; and palace exams. One could not apply for palace exams without having passed metropolitan exams, for metropolitan exams without having passed provincial exams, and for provincial exams without having passed local exams. All types of exams took place in prison-like compounds designed to curb cheating, all four involved questions regarding Confucian classics, poetry and national policy issues, and all four caused heightened anxiety in candidates [381]. The palace exam has been chosen as the most dysphoric simply because its stakes were highest. "The exams were state occasions. Held triennially, the jinshi event constituted a high point in the capital's calendar with thousands participating, thousands more providing tutorial support, sustenance and encouragement, and yet more working as invigilators, scrutineers and assessors. To preclude cheating, each candidate was installed in his own wooden cubicle. His desk by day served as his couch by night, and for better supervision the lines of cubicles lay open along one side, like a row of latrines. Responsibility for the exam rested not with the Ministry of Personnel but the Ministry of Rites, and jinshi literally meaning 'presented shi', it included a final viva in the presence of the emperor himself. Once again, ritual underlay the whole system. Scholars, just like tribute-bearers, supposedly conformed to the cosmic pattern of attraction, being drawn into the imperial orbit by its sheer centrality. Their attendance affirmed its legitimacy and their distinction added lustre to its brilliance." [382] "The anxiety from preparing for the Keju exams [which resulted in a jinshi degree] and the disappointment of doing poorly on them led to incidents of pathological behavior. Repeatedly failing the Keju exams took a severe psychological toll on the failed candidates. Effects of failure ranged from mild cases of self-doubt to various psychopathologies and, in some cases, to suicide or physical violence directed at others. In her analysis of classic exam-related novels, Judith Zeitlin found that physicians during the Ming dynasty had identified a specific form of anxiety-depression, called by these physicians siyu and referred to as 'male anxiety' by Benjamin Elman, that is associated with repeatedly failing the Keju exams. In the general literature, there have been many recorded cases of problems of delusions, illusions, and insanity attributed to failing the Keju exams repeatedly." [383]

Pathologies

♠ Corruption ♣ present ♥ It was estimated a magistrate’s annual expense varied from five to ten thousands taels, including salaries paid to private secretaries, food, daily expenses, and entertainment. These expenses were far beyond the magistrate’s nominal salary to cover. The magistrate could meet these expenses by conducting “objectionable practices” or customary fees (lougui). Members of the Chinese bureaucracy tried to collected fees from every imaginable occasion to cover their expenses. Although the fees sounded to be low and irregular, it had become widely accepted and authorized. It was within the tolerance of the law and within the custom of society. But in reality, there was often no distinction between collection of customary fees and corruption. The system had been remained the standard practice to the end of the dynasty. At the provincial levels, there were some attempts made to regulate the practice by listing fees that were permissible. Nevertheless, they never succeeded simply because all levels of officials depended on customary fees which could be charged in numerous ways. The central government had never made any attempt because it hesitated to increase the tax rate. An increase in the tax rate sounded anti-Confucian as the Chinese classical explicitly advocated low taxation to reduce burdens of the Chinese people. The Manchu government used this cultural character to control Han Chinese people because any concrete corruption would not be perceived as a systematical corruption of the government.[486]
♠ Bribery ♣ significant ♥ All the positions subordinating to the governor of prefecture could be obtained by denoting, including the positions of military officers. The implementation of denoting system complemented the revenue of the Qing dynasty but caused the expansion of the feudal bureaucratic organizations and the corruptions of the officials. Other major disadvantage of denoting system was the difficulty of achieving social stability.
