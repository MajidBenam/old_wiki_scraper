General Description[edit]

Yangshao culture (Miaodigou, Xiyincun) first developed in the Loess plateau in the Holocene period.[11] The culture was present from 5000 to 3000 BCE, extending from the Middle Yellow River Valley to modern Qinghai and Gansu.[12] Yangshao sites are mainly found in the Guanzhong region in Shaanxi, eastern Gansu, western Shanxi, southern Hebei and Henan.[13] Yangshao subsisted on wild foods and domesticated millet. Men most likely hunted, and men and women farmed and produced goods.[14]

Yangshao villages were often surrounded by a ditch, and contained groups of semi-subterranean round or square houses constructed using the wattle and daub method, a graveyard and a public courtyard.[15] Homes contained hearths for cooking and wide benches.[16] Yangshao culture is characterized by the presence of painted black and red pottery featuring animals and geometric designs.[17]Pottery, jewelry and stone, bone and ceramic tools have been excavated from Yangshao period graves.[18]

Population and political organization

In the early Yangshao phase, settlements did not have any detectable hierarchies. In the later phase, structures in the settlements began to vary in size, suggesting the existence of settlement hierarchies.[19] In many villages, a large structure is surrounded by smaller dwellings.[20] However, grave goods in Yangshao burials suggest a more egalitarian society.[21] More information is needed on settlement hierarchy and community organization in the Yangshao period. The population of Yangshao settlements varied- smaller settlements had 70 to 80 members while larger settlements housed a few hundred.[22]

"The digging of the ditches interpreted as moats to the north and south of the Xipo settlement consumed even more labor. The northern ditch from the 2005 excavation is 8m in width on the surface and 2m in width at the bottom, about 3m in depth and 300m in length. Therefore, about 4,500cum of soil was dug out to make it. Applying the same efficiency (0.4 cu m per person-day) for building the houses, the digging of the two ditches might cost 22,500 (9,000 ÷ 0.4) person-days - or 250 people working for 90 days." [49] 22,500 person-days / 365 = c.61.6 people-years

"Excavations at Xipo in western Henan yielded strong evidence for social complexity at the community level. As mentioned above, the large semi-subterranean houses there are quite complex in structure and indicate considerable labor costs. Ma (2005: 41) made an estimate of the necessary labor for the construction of structure F105. The area of the semi-subterranean part of the house is about 1,376.4 cu m. A digging experiment shows that a modern local laborer can dig a volume of 4cum per day with a steel shovel. Some scholars estimate that the efficiency of using a stone spade is about one-tenth of that using a steel spade (Zhang Bo 1987). At 0.4cum per day with a stone spade, it might take 3,441 (1,376.4 ÷ 0.4) person-days to dig the whole pit for the house. The volume of rammed earth for the foundation is about 1,023 cu m. Assuming that the labor for ramming a cubic meter of earth is twice of the labor for digging the same volume of earth (0.4 ÷ 2), it would take 5,115 (1023 ÷ 0.2) person- days to finish the ramming job. In modern Xipo village, the construction of a 50sqm traditional house usually needs 100 person-days with modern tools. Even assuming the same efficiency for the construction of the wooden framework, walls and roof, the large (516sqm) F105 would have needed another 1,032 person-days. Hence the construction of F105 might have required a total of 9,488 person-days. Considering that it is difficult for farmers to find more than three months of spare time per year without affecting agricultural production, the construction of F105 may have required more than 100 laborers working together for three months. This is obviously a large task demanding food surplus and skillful management that ordinary individuals or families could not provide. "As mentioned above, the large houses at the center of the Xipo site might have been buildings for the practice of public ceremonies. Since the houses had been internationally cleaned out when the site was abandoned, we have no evidence to know the details of those ceremonies. Yet the elite who organized the construction of these buildings might also have directed the ritual practices performed in them. The buildings are striking for their large size, central location, and absence of luxury goods. In fact there are very few luxury goods in the site as a whole. It appears that involving a large number of participants in ceremonies was more important than displaying luxury goods. Funerals were a very important component of ritual practices. Analysis of the Xipo cemetery also shows that the number of participants was more important than the quantity and quality of luxury goods. The most significant characteristic of burial M27 is its large size, as discussed above. Its burial pit of about 20cum might have needed 50 person-days to dig. The same amount of labor also was needed to prepare the mud to fill the shaft. It was even more work to prepare the wooden planks, transport them to the burial pit, and cover the chamber. Piling the large earthen mound above the burial also was an activity requiring a lot of hands. M27, the largest burial, contained no luxury goods made of materials such as jade or ivory. As a matter of fact, the pair of large-mouthed urns described above seem carelessly made. The only wealth item in the cemetery is a finely made jade yue 钺 axe beside the right arm of the deceased in burial M8. The offerings in the foot-pit for M8 are ordinary ceramic vessels except for a pair of large-mouth urns. It seems that the most important symbol of prestige during Miaodigou funerals was the ability to attract or obligate more participants." [135]

From this information it seems that the "Largest Scale Collective Ritual" was a funeral.

"A remarkable ceremonial feature of a different kind was found at another site in northern China, at the middle Yangshao period (ca. 3700-500 B.C.) site of Xishuipo in northeastern Henan (area 1). Although multiple burials continued to be the dominant form of mortuary ritual in this area, one individual was singled out for special treatment. Grave M45 of an adult male was flanked by life-sized, intricately shaped images made from clam shells, apparently representing a tiger on the right and a dragon to the left (Chang 1999; Puyang CPAM et al. 1988). The adult male lay at the southern end of the grave; three other skeletons of youths (male and female, ages 12-16) were carefully placed in the east, west, and north corners.These people may have been sacrificed to accompany the deceased to the afterlife. Feng (1990) proposes that the location of the grave was calculated according to the position of the sun during the spring and fall equinox. Therefore the animals could be figures the ancient people associated with constellations. Emerging leadership roles in this area seem linked to specialized knowledge of the heavens, and this distinctive adult male could have been a shaman (Chang 1999)." [136]

"One major feature of Yangshao worth noting is that it has yielded many remains that are indicative of shamanistic beliefs and practices. These include, for example, a skeletal (or X-ray) style of art, bisexualism in ceramic art, and, above all, the tomb of a figure, presumably religious, flanked by a dragon to his left and a tiger to his right, both formed with clam shells (Fig. 1.5). This seems to resemble descriptions in such later sources as Baopuzi (third century A.D.) of Daoist priests who employed dragons, tigers, or deer as helpers in their journeys to Heaven, where they met with deities and ancestors to acquire wisdom, medical cures, and other benefits. The Yangshao culture, however, definitely is not the only regional Neolithic culture with a shamanistic orientation. Large dragon images laid out on the ground with stones have come to light recently from both the Xinglongwa and Daxi cultures. These three cultures have very little in common in terms of material artifacts, so it is likely that shamanism is a sub- stratal feature." [137]

"Religious Beliefs. Very little is known about the religious beliefs of the Yangshao people. Some artifacts probably bore ritual significance; however, we can only speculate about their meanings. Religious Practitioners. The human face and fish motifs on the painted bowls of the Banpo phase are generally interpreted as portraits or masks of a shaman. Ceremonies. The community layouts suggest that there were at least two levels of ceremony during Yangshao times. The big houses were probably the localities where the primary level of ceremony was carried out. The cleared central courtyards indicate that they were used for the secondary level of ceremonial functions. Complete pig and dog skeletons were recovered from pits. They may represent sacrificial ceremonies." [138]

"Death and Afterlife. Death was treated with pit burial in the cemetery. A significant portion of the burials were void of burial goods. In those with burial goods, only a few used and worn-out domestic vessels and tools were offered. In the earlier phase, multiple secondary burial was quite popular. Biological relatives were reunited after dead, regardless of their sex. There was also indication of mutilation of body parts during the burial rituals. Bones of the extremities and phalanges were recovered from offering vessels or in grave fillings. Some bones had been dyed with natural red ocher. Infants and children were buried in large red-ware pottery jars in the dwelling area, usually not in the cemetery. Painted bowls were used to cover the receptacles. Occasionally, small holes were intentionally punctured in the bottoms of the bowls. They were probably used for the release of a children's spirits. Sometimes, adult dead were treated with secondary urn burial. Bones were collected from a previous treatment phase and deposited in large urns." [139]
