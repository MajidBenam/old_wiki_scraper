General Description[edit]

In the Greek world, the Hellenistic era goes from the death of Alexander in 323 BCE to the Roman conquest in 69 BCE. For much of this period, due to its enviable position in the Eastern Mediterranean, the island lay at the centre international conflicts between Alexander's successors, most notably the Ptolemies (who managed to establish an autonomous republic at Itanos in the third century[2]), the Seleucids, Macedonia, and Pergamon.[3] This in turn exacerbated conflicts between the island's chief city-states, with few periods of respite, until Crete was conquered by the Romans[4].

Population and political organization

In terms of the island's population at this time, estimates vary for a minimum of 200,000 to a maximum of 1,000,000 people; however, the most likely estimate is of 450,000-500,000 people. [5] Political, military and religious control in Cretan city-states was exercised by the Kosmoi (Κόσμοι), a board of 3 to 10 nobles, annually elected by the Ecclesia, the body of free male citizens. One of the Kosmoi, known as protokosmos or stratagetas, was the president of the board. The council of elders, the Gerousia, whose members were chosen among the best Kosmoi, had legislative and juridical authority.[6][7]

Measurement System[edit]

♠ Length ♣ present♥ The measurements for the length are based on daktylos (δάκτυλος) for smaller units and pous (πούς) and station (στάδιον) for larger units. The values of these units varied according to area and period but the relative proportions were generally the same: daktylos: 19.3 mm, pous: 0.308 m. and station: 185.4 m. Length was measured by a cord of some standard length (schoinion), a metallic chain (halysis), or a rod from reed or wood (kalamos). [38]
♠ Volume ♣ present♥ Greeks measured liquid capacity by the metretes (34 liters) and dry capacity by the medimnos (25 mgr). Each measure was based on the common unit of the kotyle. The values of these units varied according to area and period but the relative proportions were generally the same. [39]
♠ Weight ♣ present♥ There were two dominant weight standards in the Greek world, the Euboic (introduced to Attica by Solon) and the Aeginetic. The weights, their names bear a nominal resemblance to currency, are the obol (0.72 g in Attic/Euboic and 1.05g in Aeginetic standard), drachma (6 obols: 4.31g in Attic/Euboic and 6.3g in Aeginetic standard), mina (100 drachmae: 431g in Attic/Euboic and 630g in Aeginetic standard), and talent (60 mine: 25.86 kg in Attic/Euboic and 37.8 kg in Aeginetic standard).[40] The values of these units show a great variation according to area and period. The Cretan weight system was based on the Aeginetic standard.
♠ Time ♣ present♥ In the Greek world, the day began after sunset. [41] The day, divided in units, was measured by sundials (scathe dials or ring dials) and during the night by water clocks (clepsydrae). Cretans named each year after the president of the board of Kosmoi (protokosmos or kosmos o epi poleos). In Hellenistic times, years were also reckoned in quadrennial periods according to the Olympiads. The year, started in the summer solstice, was divided into twelve months: Agyios (December-January), Dioskouros (January-February), Theodosios (February-March), Pontos (March-April), Rabinthios (April-May), Hyperberetos (May-June), Nekysios (June-July), Basileios (July-August), Thersmofhorion (August-September), Hermaios (September-October), Metarhios (October-November), Eiman (November-December). ♠ Expert ♣ Kostis Christakis♥

General Description of Warfare: The Hellenistic period is marked by constant warfare between the city states, the main protagonists being Knossos and Gortyn, around which two powerful, if changeable, alliances formed. As Polybius said "For of the constant succession of their civil wars and their excessive cruelty to each other, beginning and end mean the same thing in Crete, and what is regarded as a paradoxical utterance of some philosophers is there constantly a matter of fact. [58] The basic causes of conflict were land acquisition, expansion of the great cities’ hegemony and control of important sanctuaries. [59] The clashes either spread across Crete (221-219, 200-193, 189, 184, 121-111 BCE) or were limited to specific areas. A new element was the much more frequent involvement of foreign powers and large-scale wars. The complex and changeable relationships and conflicts of the great Hellenistic powers, mainly between the Ptolemies on the one hand and the Seleucids and Antigonids on the other, and Rome’s aspirations to control the East Mediterranean after the 2nd century BCE, affected the political situation in Crete. The island’s status as a vitally important hub of maritime communications meant that the foreign powers needed to control its harbours. Crete was also the main source of mercenaries, while it controlled the East Mediterranean with its naval forces, and its pirate ships were a constant threat. The military technology of the time was that adopted by the Greek city-states during the Hellenistic period. Our excavation data, however, from Crete are relatively limited. Increasing political instability contributed to the increase of the Cretan piracy. [60] Etruscan and other Italia pirates lost their dominance (early in the 3rd century BCE) and the Cretans began to be the principal marauders. The principal states of the period made use of the pirate forces in a systematic way. It was during the course of the 2nd century BCE that Cretan piracy declined, largely due to the efficiency of the counter-measures adopted by Rhodes.

♠ Name ♣ Ekdysia or Periblemata ♥ Ekdysia or Periblemata is essentially a single ritual. [170] The ceremony combined elements of fertility, initiation and marriage-ritual and clearly have roots in the earliest stratum of Cretan religion. For the youth were apparently initiated into manhood, citizenship and marriage at the same period of life. When growth was achieved, the boy died as a boy and was born again as a man, cast aside his boyhood costume and wore mans clothes. The ritual, took place during the spring, culminated in a festival in which the young men put of the garments of ephebic age (Ekdysia) and received the garment of the citizen (Periblemata). The ritual was followed by solemn oaths, promising to protect their city, defend their allies and fight agains their enemies, feasts, and collective marriages. The organization of these rites was on the responsibility of the state institutions. It is very like that these rites goes back to the Bronze Age period.[171]

This suggests that the development of the idea of the purity of the mind and its priority over the purity of the body was part of a more general process of fundamental changes in the perception of rituals in Classical and Hellenistic Greece.
